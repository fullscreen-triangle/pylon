\begin{abstract}
% =====================================================================
\noindent
We present a control system for stirred-tank bioreactors built on a
strict architectural separation between two layers that most designs
conflate. The \emph{control layer} normalises every heterogeneous
process channel---dissolved oxygen, pH, temperature, agitation, feed
rate---onto a common bounded coordinate hypercube, partitions that
hypercube into a finite set of \emph{cells}, and dispatches a
pre-compiled action from the cell that the current normalised state
occupies. We prove that this quantised controller is globally
asymptotically stable under an explicit piecewise Lyapunov condition,
that its discrete-time operator is a contraction with geometric
convergence, that its cell width is bounded above by a sampling
(Nyquist-type) criterion tied to the process bandwidth, and that it is
\emph{structurally incorruptible}: because the controller consumes only
a scalar timing/registry index and dispatches from a fixed finite action
set, no sensor payload can drive the plant outside that set. The
\emph{monitoring layer} sits strictly downstream of the control layer
across a one-directional interface. It treats each measurement not as a
number but as one \emph{confirmation} in a redundant ensemble: sensors
are modelled as coupled oscillators that federate by phase entrainment,
and a process excursion is confirmed only when independent channels
agree, with a decline-to-safe-state outcome when they irreducibly
disagree. Because observation and computation are the same
operation---both place a value into a cell---the system runs two
concurrent scripts, a sensor script and a \emph{metabolic} script, the
latter synthesising \emph{virtual sensors}: channels that no probe
measures but that are computed at a node from several physical channels
(for example a respiratory-state channel assembled from pH, off-gas
$\mathrm{CO_2}$, and temperature) and then serve as genuine control
coordinates, made safe-by-construction by a certified fallback value. We
derive the \emph{sensor synthesis map} that produces such channels: an
information-preserving bijection switches each measurement between a
time-domain value and a frequency-domain oscillator phase, inputs are
combined in the frequency domain under a mean-recovery (circular-mean)
constraint, and the result is switched back to a time-domain cell---one
operation yielding both an actionable synthesised value and, as the
concentration of the combination, a trustworthy ensemble invariant.
Among computed channels we distinguish \emph{ensemble invariants}:
quantities invariant under relabelling of interchangeable units and
realised by no single unit---information true of the whole sensor set and
present in none of its members---which we prove are robust to any
single-unit fault by an $O(1/n)$ bound and which detect exactly the
correlated failures that redundant probes cannot. We
show that the monitoring layer is \emph{additive}: every
one of its guarantees can fail---sensors drop out, the ensemble
decoheres, confirmation goes silent---without affecting the control
layer's stability or safety proofs, because the interface is
one-directional and the control law never depends on any monitoring
output for correctness. In the worst case the system degrades
continuously to an ordinary provably-stable single-channel cell-partition
controller. We give the complete state-space model, stability proofs, a
CUSUM-equivalent excursion-detection identity, a federation admission
criterion, six algorithms, and a validation protocol on a fed-batch
\textit{Saccharomyces cerevisiae} process. The framework is
self-contained: every claim is proved from stated definitions using only
standard results in nonlinear control, information theory, and
synchronisation.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} bioreactor control; cell-partition
quantisation; piecewise Lyapunov stability; S-normalisation;
dissolved-oxygen control; CUSUM; sensor federation; Kuramoto
synchronisation; virtual soft sensors; structural incorruptibility;
fail-safe degradation.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{The problem}

A stirred-tank bioreactor is a small chemical plant whose product is
living cells or the molecules they secrete. Its controller must regulate
a handful of coupled variables---dissolved oxygen (DO), pH, temperature,
agitation speed, and one or more feed rates---each measured in
incommensurable units and each coupled to the others through the
metabolism of the culture~\citep{doran2013bioprocess,shuler2017bioprocess}.
Three difficulties recur across the bioprocess-control
literature~\citep{alford2006bioprocess,mandenius2016challenges}. First,
\emph{dimensional heterogeneity}: composing a DO channel (percent of
saturation) with a temperature channel (kelvin) and a feed channel
(litres per hour) requires unit bookkeeping at every algebraic step, and
errors in that bookkeeping are a documented source of instability.
Second, \emph{sensor unreliability}: the DO and pH probes that dominate
bioprocess feedback drift, foul, and fail, and a controller that trusts a
single probe inherits that probe's failure
modes~\citep{garcia2009review,luttmann2012softsensors}. Third,
\emph{safety and integrity}: a networked controller for a
pharmaceutical-grade process is a cyber-physical system whose failure or
compromise is costly, and the regulatory framework demands that process
excursions be detected and that the system fail to a defined safe
state~\citep{fda2004pat,cardenas2008secure}.

The three difficulties pull in different directions. Richer sensing
improves observability but enlarges the attack and failure surface.
Tighter multivariable control improves regulation but couples the
controller's correctness to the reliability of every channel it reads. A
design that fuses all sensors into one state estimate and feeds that
estimate to one control law is only as trustworthy as its least reliable
sensor.

\subsection{The thesis: separate the layers}

This paper argues that the correct response is an architectural
separation, enforced by a one-directional interface, between a
\emph{control layer} and a \emph{monitoring layer}.

The control layer is deliberately austere. It maps every physical
channel onto a common bounded coordinate---we call this
\emph{S-normalisation}---so the working state lives in a
dimensionless hypercube $\Hs = [0,1]^{m}$. It partitions $\Hs$ into a
finite set of cells and stores, for each cell, one pre-compiled control
action. At each step it determines which cell the current normalised
state occupies and dispatches that cell's action. Nothing else. This
austerity is what makes the layer provable: we establish global
asymptotic stability by a piecewise Lyapunov argument, geometric
convergence by a contraction argument, a cell-width bound by a
sampling criterion, and---because the dispatch depends only on
cell membership and the action set is fixed at compile time---structural
incorruptibility, meaning no measurement value can cause an action
outside the pre-approved set.

The monitoring layer is where richness lives, and it is placed entirely
downstream. It reads the same physical channels, but instead of
producing a state estimate that the controller must trust, it produces
\emph{confirmations}: each measurement is one vote in a redundant
ensemble that agrees or dissents about whether the process has entered a
given region. Sensors federate---each is modelled as an oscillator whose
frequency is perturbed by what it measures, and the ensemble reaches
agreement by phase entrainment rather than by exchanging content. When
the ensemble is coherent, agreement is automatic and cheap; when it is
not, the layer degrades gracefully and, in the limit of disagreement,
declines to a safe state. Crucially, the monitoring layer's outputs enter
the control layer only through the same narrow interface any operator
would use---as a request to change setpoint or enter a safe mode---never
as a term in the control law. It is therefore \emph{additive}: it can
only improve confirmation quality and diagnostic resolution, and it
cannot break the control-layer proofs, because those proofs do not
reference it.

\subsection{The worst-case guarantee}

The value of the separation is a precise worst-case statement, which we
prove in \Cref{sec:separation}: \emph{if every component of the
monitoring layer fails, the system reduces to an ordinary
single-channel cell-partition controller, which remains globally
asymptotically stable and structurally incorruptible.} There is no
scenario in which the speculative or elaborate parts of the monitoring
layer are load-bearing for stability or safety. This is what licenses
building the monitoring layer ambitiously: its failure is bounded by the
control layer's floor.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{S-normalisation and the cell-partition control law}
(\Cref{sec:foundations,sec:control}). A dimensionless bounded state space
$\Hs=[0,1]^m$, a finite cell partition, and a nearest-centroid
proportional dispatch that is $L^2$-optimal among piecewise-constant
laws.
\item \textbf{Stability and convergence} (\Cref{sec:stability}).
Piecewise Lyapunov global asymptotic stability reducible to per-cell
linear matrix inequalities; a Banach contraction with explicit geometric
rate; and a Nyquist-type cell-width bound tied to process bandwidth.
\item \textbf{Structural incorruptibility} (\Cref{sec:security}). A proof
that no measurement payload has a path to an action outside the
pre-compiled set, and a bounded-effect analysis of spurious or delayed
sensor events.
\item \textbf{The confirmation calculus} (\Cref{sec:monitoring}). A
CUSUM-equivalent identity linking cell-boundary crossings to
out-of-control alarms; a multiplicative composition law for independent
sensor confirmations; a coherence requirement forcing at least three
independent channels for robust grounding; and a
convergent/decline dichotomy for honest termination.
\item \textbf{Sensor federation} (\Cref{sec:federation}). A temporal-agent
model in which measurements join an ensemble by oscillator perturbation,
a critical-coupling threshold for phase lock, a zero-overhead theorem at
coherence, and an information-theoretic admission criterion.
\item \textbf{Virtual sensors, ensemble invariants, and the two-script
model} (\Cref{sec:virtual}). Using the equivalence of observation and

grid, with an $O(1/n)$ single-unit-fault robustness bound for invariants.
\item \textbf{The sensor synthesis map and the time--frequency switch}
(\Cref{sec:switch,sec:synthesis}). A derived, information-preserving
bijection between a channel's time-domain value and its frequency-domain
phase that respects the cell partition, and a synthesis operation
(frequency-domain circular mean under mean-recovery, switched back to a
cell) that generates the value and the invariant of a computed channel
together.
\item \textbf{The separation theorem and fail-safe degradation}
(\Cref{sec:separation}). A formal proof that the monitoring layer is
additive and that worst-case behaviour is an ordinary stable controller.
\item \textbf{Bioreactor realisation} (\Cref{sec:bioreactor,sec:algorithms}).
The full architecture, channel definitions, six algorithms, and a
validation protocol on a fed-batch yeast process.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Foundations: bounded state, normalisation, and cells}
\label{sec:foundations}
% =====================================================================

The control layer operates on a normalised, bounded, finitely-partitioned
state space. This section constructs that space, records the elementary
facts we will use, and derives the resolution floor---the strictly
positive lower bound on how finely any physical channel can be resolved,
which every later bound refers back to.

\subsection{Physical channels and S-normalisation}

\begin{definition}[Process channel]
\label{def:channel}
A \emph{process channel} is a measured or manipulated physical quantity
$q\in\R$ of the bioreactor with a known operating range
$[q_{\min},q_{\max}]$, $q_{\min}<q_{\max}$. For a stirred-tank reactor the
channels are dissolved oxygen, pH, temperature, agitation rate, and one
or more feed rates (\Cref{sec:bioreactor}).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[S-coordinate]
\label{def:snorm}
The \emph{S-coordinate} of a channel value $q$ is the affine
normalisation onto the unit interval,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:snorm}
S(q)\;=\;\frac{q-q_{\min}}{q_{\max}-q_{\min}}\;\in\;[0,1],
\end{equation}
with inverse $q=q_{\min}+S(q)\,(q_{\max}-q_{\min})$. For an $m$-channel
reactor the \emph{S-state vector} is
$\Ss=(S(q_1),\dots,S(q_m))^{\top}\in[0,1]^m$.
\end{definition}

The single purpose of \eqref{eq:snorm} is to remove dimension. After
normalisation every channel lives in the same dimensionless interval, so
channels of incommensurable physical units can be compared, composed, and
partitioned uniformly, with no conversion factors. This is the resolution
of the dimensional-heterogeneity difficulty of \Cref{sec:intro}.

\begin{definition}[S-state space]
\label{def:hspace}
The \emph{S-state space} is the hypercube
$\Hs=[0,1]^m\subset\R^m$ with the Euclidean norm
$\norm{\Ss}=\bigl(\sum_{i=1}^m S_i^2\bigr)^{1/2}$ and the metric
$d(\Ss,\Ss')=\norm{\Ss-\Ss'}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Compact complete metric space]
\label{prop:complete}
$(\Hs,d)$ is a compact, connected, complete metric space, and it carries
the Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ of $\R^m$ restricted to $\Hs$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\Hs=[0,1]^m$ is a closed and bounded subset of $\R^m$, hence compact by
Heine--Borel and complete as a closed subset of the complete space
$(\R^m,\norm{\cdot})$~\citep{munkres2000topology}. It is a product of
connected intervals, hence connected. Restriction of Lebesgue measure to
a Borel set is a measure. \qed
\end{proof}

Compactness and completeness are exactly what the extreme-value,
finite-cover, and fixed-point arguments of \Cref{sec:stability} require;
we invoke this proposition without further comment there.

\subsection{The cell partition}

\begin{definition}[Cell partition]
\label{def:cells}
A \emph{cell partition} of $\Hs$ is a finite family
$\Cs=\{C_1,\dots,C_N\}$ of Borel sets with
(i)~$\bigcup_{i=1}^N C_i=\Hs$;
(ii)~$C_i\cap C_j=\varnothing$ for $i\neq j$; and
(iii)~$\lambda(C_i)>0$ for every $i$.
Each $C_i$ is a \emph{cell}. For axis-aligned rectangular cells, the
\emph{width} of the partition is the smallest cell side length along any
coordinate,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:width}
\wcell\;=\;\min_{i}\ \min_{1\le k\le m}\,\mathrm{len}_k(C_i),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{len}_k(C_i)$ is the extent of cell $C_i$ along axis $k$. A
partition is \emph{$\wcell$-regular} if every cell contains a
coordinate-aligned box of side $\ge\wcell$ in each dimension.
\end{definition}

For the reactor we use axis-aligned rectangular cells (a grid, possibly
non-uniform), for which the width is simply the smallest grid spacing
along any axis. Rectangular cells make cell lookup an $O(m)$ operation
(one comparison per axis) and make the per-cell stability tests of
\Cref{sec:stability} tractable.

\begin{definition}[Cell of a state; cell map]
\label{def:cellmap}
For $\Ss\in\Hs$ let $\mathrm{cell}(\Ss)$ denote the unique cell containing
$\Ss$ (ties on measure-zero boundaries broken by a fixed rule, e.g.\
half-open cells). The \emph{cell map} $\mathrm{cell}\colon\Hs\to\Cs$ is
measurable.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Cell centroid]
\label{def:centroid}
The \emph{centroid} of cell $C_i$ is
\begin{equation}
\bar\Ss_i\;=\;\frac{1}{\lambda(C_i)}\int_{C_i}\Ss\,\mathrm d\lambda\;\in\;\overline{C_i}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsection{The resolution floor}

No physical instrument resolves a channel to a point. Every sensor and
every actuator has a finite resolution $\delta>0$ below which two values
are indistinguishable, set by quantisation, noise, or the uncertainty of
the transduction. After S-normalisation this resolution becomes a
dimensionless floor on cell width.

\begin{assumption}[Finite resolution]
\label{ass:resolution}
Each channel $k$ has a finite instrument resolution $\delta_k>0$: values
of $q_k$ differing by less than $\delta_k(q_{k,\max}-q_{k,\min})$ in raw
units are not distinguishable by the reactor's transducers. Consequently
no realisable cell partition has width below
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:floor}
\floor\;:=\;\min_{1\le k\le m}\delta_k\;>\;0 .
\end{equation}
The constant $\floor$ is the \emph{resolution floor}.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[The floor is strictly positive and bounds every cell]
\label{prop:floor}
Under \Cref{ass:resolution}, every $\wcell$-regular realisable partition
has $\wcell\ge\floor>0$, and every cell has Lebesgue measure
$\lambda(C_i)\ge\floor^{\,m}>0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A realisable partition cannot separate two states that the instruments
cannot distinguish; hence no cell side can be shorter than the smallest
distinguishable increment $\floor$ of \eqref{eq:floor}, giving
$\wcell\ge\floor$. A $\wcell$-regular cell contains a coordinate-aligned
box of side $\ge\wcell\ge\floor$ in each of $m$ dimensions, whose measure
is $\ge\floor^{\,m}$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the floor matters]
\label{rem:floor-role}
The floor is the single quantity that makes the control layer finite and
therefore provable. Because $\lambda(C_i)\ge\floor^{\,m}$ and
$\lambda(\Hs)=1$, the number of cells is bounded,
$N\le\floor^{-m}<\infty$ (\Cref{lem:finite-cells}), so the action set is
finite; because $\wcell\ge\floor$, the sampling bound of
\Cref{thm:nyquist} is non-vacuous; and because no separation is finer
than $\floor$, a state's cell membership is a robust, noise-tolerant
datum rather than a fragile point estimate. Every guarantee in
\Cref{sec:stability,sec:security} ultimately rests on $\floor>0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Finite cell count]
\label{lem:finite-cells}
Under \Cref{ass:resolution}, any realisable partition has
$N\le\lambda(\Hs)/\floor^{\,m}=\floor^{-m}<\infty$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The cells are disjoint with $\sum_i\lambda(C_i)=\lambda(\Hs)=1$ and each
$\lambda(C_i)\ge\floor^{\,m}$ by \Cref{prop:floor}; hence
$N\floor^{\,m}\le1$. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Two views of a normalised state: the time--frequency switch}
\label{sec:switch}

The same S-state admits two descriptions, one used by each layer, and the
map between them---the \emph{sensor synthesis map}---is the operational
form of the identity ``observation $=$ computation'' developed in
\Cref{sec:virtual}. The control layer names a state in the
\emph{time domain}: by its cell index $i$ with $\Ss\in C_i$, reconstructed
when needed to the centroid $\bar\Ss_i$; equivalently, per channel, by the
inter-pulse interval $\DP$ of \Cref{sec:federation}. The monitoring layer
names a state in the \emph{frequency domain}: by an oscillator phase. We
derive the switch between the two views, prove it is an
information-preserving bijection that respects the cell partition, and
show it is the substrate on which sensor synthesis
(\Cref{sec:synthesis,alg:synth}) operates.

\begin{definition}[Phase representation]
\label{def:phase}
The \emph{phase} of a normalised channel value $S\in[0,1]$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:phase}
\Phi(S)\;=\;2\pi S\;\in\;[0,2\pi),
\end{equation}
the angular position of a reference oscillator whose one full turn spans
the channel's operating range. The value $S$ is the \emph{time-domain}
coordinate (a position in $[0,1]$, equivalently a cell index and offset);
$\phi=\Phi(S)$ is the \emph{frequency-domain} coordinate (an oscillator
phase).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[The switch is an information-preserving bijection]
\label{prop:switch}
The map $\Phi\colon[0,1]\to[0,2\pi)$ of \eqref{eq:phase} is an
order-preserving bijection with inverse $\Phi^{-1}(\phi)=\phi/2\pi$; both
$\Phi$ and $\Phi^{-1}$ are Lipschitz \textup{(}constants $2\pi$ and
$1/2\pi$\textup{)} and measurable. Consequently the time-domain and
frequency-domain descriptions of a channel carry identical information,
and the switch loses none.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\Phi(S)=2\pi S$ is affine with positive slope, hence a strictly
increasing continuous bijection of $[0,1]$ onto $[0,2\pi)$ with continuous
inverse $\phi/2\pi$; affine maps are Lipschitz with the stated constants
and Borel measurable. A bijection with measurable inverse transports the
Borel $\sigma$-algebra of one interval onto the other, so no measurable
event is created or destroyed: the two coordinates encode the same
information. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[The switch respects the cell partition]
\label{prop:switch-cells}
The image under $\Phi$ of a one-dimensional cell $[a,b]\subseteq[0,1]$ is
the phase arc $[2\pi a,2\pi b)$, and $\Phi$ carries the channel's cell
partition onto a partition of the phase circle into arcs of equal count.
Hence cell membership is the same predicate in either domain: a
time-domain cell test and a frequency-domain arc test return the same
cell.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\Phi$ is an increasing bijection, so it maps the interval $[a,b]$ onto
$[2\pi a,2\pi b)$ and maps a partition of $[0,1]$ into intervals onto the
partition of $[0,2\pi)$ into the corresponding arcs, preserving order and
disjointness. Membership $S\in[a,b]\iff\phi\in[2\pi a,2\pi b)$ by
monotonicity. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Consistency with the frequency-perturbation form]
\label{cor:switch-freq}
The frequency-perturbation encoding of a measurement used by the
federation, $\DP=2\pi/(\omega+\delta\omega)$
\textup{(}\Cref{sec:federation}\textup{)}, is the time-domain image of the
phase view: a measured shift $\delta\omega$ of the oscillator frequency
advances the phase, and reading the phase back through $\Phi^{-1}$
recovers the S-value and hence the cell. The control layer therefore never
needs the phase view---it reads $S$ directly---while the monitoring layer
uses it only to federate sensors \textup{(}\Cref{sec:federation}\textup{)}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Over one reference period the accumulated phase is
$\phi=\int(\omega+\delta\omega)\,\mathrm dt$; normalising by $2\pi$ and
applying $\Phi^{-1}$ returns the corresponding $S\in[0,1]$, whose cell is
the same in both domains by \Cref{prop:switch-cells}. The inter-pulse
interval $\DP=2\pi/(\omega+\delta\omega)$ is the time-domain reading of the
same oscillator. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{prop:switch,prop:switch-cells,cor:switch-freq} establish the switch
as a lossless, cell-preserving bijection between a time-domain value and a
frequency-domain phase. This is what lets the two layers use different
views of one state without either losing information, and---crucially---it
is the substrate for \emph{synthesising} new channels by combining phases
and switching back, which we derive in \Cref{sec:synthesis} once the
virtual-channel machinery is in place. The clean split of views is also
what the separation of \Cref{sec:separation} formalises: the control layer
lives entirely in the time domain, the federation entirely in the
frequency domain, and the synthesis map is the only bridge.

\section{The cell-partition control law}
\label{sec:control}
% =====================================================================

We now define the control layer. It is a quantised feedback law in the
sense of Brockett and Liberzon~\citep{brockett2000quantized}: a
finite-valued control that stabilises a continuous-state plant. The
novelty for bioprocessing is that the partition is defined on the
S-normalised hypercube, so a single law serves all channels uniformly,
and that the design is arranged so the monitoring layer can be removed
without disturbing it.

\subsection{The plant model}

We model the closed-loop reactor dynamics in the S-state space. Write
$\Ss(t)\in\Hs$ for the normalised state and $\mathbf u\in\Us$ for the
applied control from a finite control set $\Us=\{u_1,\dots,u_N\}$.

\begin{assumption}[Cellwise-Lipschitz dynamics]
\label{ass:dynamics}
The closed-loop S-dynamics are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:plant}
\dot{\Ss}\;=\;\mathbf F\bigl(\Ss,\,\mathbf u\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf F\colon\Hs\times\Us\to\R^m$ is Lipschitz in $\Ss$ on each
cell $C_i$ (with possibly cell-dependent Lipschitz constant $L_i$), and
$\Hs$ is forward-invariant: trajectories starting in $\Hs$ remain in
$\Hs$ (guaranteed physically by the saturation of every actuator at the
channel limits $q_{\min},q_{\max}$).
\end{assumption}

Forward invariance is not an idealisation: it holds because the
normalisation \eqref{eq:snorm} clamps each channel to $[0,1]$ by
construction, and the physical actuators saturate at the same limits.

\subsection{The cell-action map and the control law}

\begin{definition}[Cell-action map]
\label{def:cellaction}
A \emph{cell-action map} is a function $A\colon\Cs\to\Us$ assigning to
each cell one pre-compiled control action. The induced state-feedback law
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:controllaw}
\mathbf u(t)\;=\;A\bigl(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss(t))\bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The controller's entire runtime behaviour is \eqref{eq:controllaw}:
measure $\Ss$, find its cell, emit that cell's stored action. The action
is pre-compiled and pre-verified offline; at runtime no arithmetic on the
raw measurement, no parsing of any payload, and no search occurs. This
austerity is the source of both the incorruptibility of
\Cref{sec:security} and the additivity of \Cref{sec:separation}.

\begin{definition}[Nearest-centroid proportional map]
\label{def:ncp}
Given a setpoint $\Sstar\in\Hs$ and a scalar gain $K>0$, the
\emph{nearest-centroid proportional} cell-action map is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ncp}
u_i\;=\;K\bigl(\Sstar-\bar\Ss_i\bigr),\qquad i=1,\dots,N,
\end{equation}
i.e.\ each cell drives the state from its centroid toward the setpoint.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[$L^2$-optimality of the nearest-centroid map]
\label{prop:optimal}
Among all piecewise-constant maps $A\colon\Cs\to\R^m$ that are constant on
each cell, the nearest-centroid map \eqref{eq:ncp} minimises the
$L^2(\Hs,\lambda)$ deviation from the ideal proportional law
$K(\Sstar-\Ss)$:
\begin{equation}
A^\star=\arg\min_{A}\int_{\Hs}\bigl\lVert K(\Sstar-\Ss)-A(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss))\bigr\rVert^2\,\mathrm d\lambda .
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The objective separates over cells, since the cells partition $\Hs$. On a
fixed cell $C_i$ the value $u_i$ minimising
$\int_{C_i}\lVert K(\Sstar-\Ss)-u_i\rVert^2\,\mathrm d\lambda$ is found by
setting the derivative in $u_i$ to zero:
$u_i=\frac{1}{\lambda(C_i)}\int_{C_i}K(\Sstar-\Ss)\,\mathrm d\lambda
=K(\Sstar-\bar\Ss_i)$, the centroid rule \eqref{eq:ncp}. Minimising each
cell's integral minimises the sum. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Discrete-time realisation}

A digital controller applies \eqref{eq:controllaw} at sampling instants.
Let $h>0$ be the sampling period and define the discrete-time operator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discop}
T(\Ss)\;=\;\Ss+h\,\mathbf F\bigl(\Ss,\,A(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss))\bigr),
\end{equation}
the forward-Euler step of \eqref{eq:plant} under the cell-partition law.
The closed-loop trajectory is the orbit $\Ss_{k+1}=T(\Ss_k)$. The
sampling period $h$ is tied to the reference-oscillator/pulse rate
$\fref=1/h$ used by the monitoring layer (\Cref{sec:federation}), but the
control law's correctness depends only on the bound of
\Cref{thm:nyquist} and \Cref{thm:banach}, never on the monitoring layer.

\subsection{The committed-step record}

The controller maintains a single monotone counter, used later for
excursion accounting and replay resistance.

\begin{definition}[Committed record]
\label{def:record}
The \emph{committed record} $M\in\N$ is the number of control steps taken
since initialisation. It increases by one at each application of
\eqref{eq:discop} and is never decremented.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Monotonicity and non-return]
\label{prop:monotone}
$M$ is strictly increasing in step index. Consequently the controller
never returns to a prior \emph{state} $(\,\Ss,M\,)$ even when it revisits
a prior S-value: for step indices $j>i$, $(\Ss_j,M_j)\neq(\Ss_i,M_i)$
because $M_j>M_i$, regardless of whether $\Ss_j=\Ss_i$. An operation
intended to undo a prior step is itself a step and increments $M$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each step increments $M$ by exactly one (\Cref{def:record}), so
$M_j=M_i+(j-i)>M_i$ for $j>i$; the second components of the two states
differ, so the states differ. An ``undo'' is an application of
\eqref{eq:discop}, hence a further increment. \qed
\end{proof}

The record makes ``the same reading again'' distinguishable from ``the
original reading,'' which is exactly what a replay defence and an
excursion audit require (\Cref{sec:security}). It costs one integer and
is never load-bearing for stability.

\section{Stability, convergence, and the sampling bound}
\label{sec:stability}
% =====================================================================

This section establishes the three quantitative guarantees of the
control layer: global asymptotic stability of the setpoint, geometric
convergence of the discrete-time loop, and an upper bound on cell width
required for faithful regulation. All three are proved from the
definitions of \Cref{sec:foundations,sec:control} using standard
results, and all three refer back to the resolution floor $\floor>0$.

\subsection{Piecewise Lyapunov stability}

\begin{theorem}[Piecewise Lyapunov global asymptotic stability]
\label{thm:pwlyap}
Suppose there is a continuous $V\colon\Hs\to\R_{\ge0}$ and class
$\mathcal K_\infty$ functions $\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3$ such that for
all $\Ss\in\Hs$,
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item $\alpha_1(\norm{\Ss-\Sstar})\le V(\Ss)\le\alpha_2(\norm{\Ss-\Sstar})$;
\item on every cell $C_i$, along \eqref{eq:plant} with
$\mathbf u=A(C_i)$,
$\dot V(\Ss)\le-\alpha_3(\norm{\Ss-\Sstar})$.
\end{enumerate}
Then the setpoint $\Sstar$ is globally asymptotically stable for the
closed loop on $\Hs$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Condition~(i) makes $V$ positive definite and radially unbounded relative
to $\Sstar$ on $\Hs$~\citep{khalil2002nonlinear}. By~(ii), on each cell
$V$ strictly decreases along trajectories; since the finitely many cells
cover $\Hs$ (\Cref{lem:finite-cells}) and $V$ is continuous across the
measure-zero cell boundaries, the decrease holds along any trajectory,
which crosses only finitely many cells in finite time. Integrating,
$V(\Ss(t))-V(\Ss(0))=\int_0^t\dot V\,\mathrm d\tau
\le-\int_0^t\alpha_3(\norm{\Ss(\tau)-\Sstar})\,\mathrm d\tau$. Because
$\Hs$ is compact and forward-invariant (\Cref{ass:dynamics}), the
trajectory is defined for all $t\ge0$ and remains in $\Hs$. Standard
Lyapunov theory then gives $V(\Ss(t))\to0$ and, by the lower bound
in~(i), $\norm{\Ss(t)-\Sstar}\to0$, which is global asymptotic
stability~\citep{khalil2002nonlinear}. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Quadratic sufficiency: a per-cell LMI]
\label{cor:lmi}
Take $V(\Ss)=\norm{\Ss-\Sstar}^2$. Then the conditions of
\Cref{thm:pwlyap} hold if, on each cell $C_i$ with local linearisation
$\mathbf A_i=\partial\mathbf F/\partial\Ss\big|_{C_i}$ (under action
$u_i$), there is $\alpha_i>0$ with
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lmi}
\mathbf A_i+\mathbf A_i^{\top}\;\preceq\;-\alpha_i\,I .
\end{equation}
Global asymptotic stability then reduces to checking the finite family of
linear matrix inequalities \eqref{eq:lmi}, one per cell, solvable by
standard semidefinite programming~\citep{johansson1998computation}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
With $V=\norm{\Ss-\Sstar}^2$, $\dot V=2(\Ss-\Sstar)^{\top}\mathbf F$.
Linearising $\mathbf F\approx\mathbf A_i(\Ss-\Sstar)$ on $C_i$ gives
$\dot V\approx(\Ss-\Sstar)^{\top}(\mathbf A_i+\mathbf A_i^{\top})(\Ss-\Sstar)$,
which is $\le-\alpha_i\norm{\Ss-\Sstar}^2$ exactly when \eqref{eq:lmi}
holds. Taking $\alpha_3(r)=(\min_i\alpha_i)\,r^2$ and
$\alpha_1(r)=\alpha_2(r)=r^2$ satisfies \Cref{thm:pwlyap}. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{cor:lmi} is the practical stability certificate: after choosing the
grid, one solves $N$ small LMIs offline; if all pass, the closed loop is
certified globally asymptotically stable before the reactor is ever run.
The nearest-centroid map \eqref{eq:ncp} makes each $\mathbf A_i$ a stable
proportional term $-KI$ plus the plant's cellwise Jacobian, so
\eqref{eq:lmi} holds whenever the gain $K$ dominates the plant's local
expansion---a familiar, checkable condition.

\subsection{Geometric convergence: a contraction}

\begin{theorem}[Banach contraction and geometric convergence]
\label{thm:banach}
Suppose the piecewise Lyapunov descent of \Cref{thm:pwlyap} holds with
rate $\alpha>0$ \textup{(}i.e.\ $\alpha=\min_i\alpha_i$\textup{)}, and
$\norm{\partial\mathbf F/\partial\Ss}\le L_F$ uniformly on $\Hs$. If the
sampling period satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:step}
0<h<\frac{2\alpha}{L_F^2},
\end{equation}
then the discrete-time operator $T$ of \eqref{eq:discop} is a contraction
on $(\Hs,d)$ with constant $\rho=\sqrt{1-2h\alpha+h^2L_F^2}<1$. By the
Banach fixed-point theorem~\citep{kreyszig1989functional} $T$ has a
unique fixed point $\Sstar$, and for every $\Ss_0\in\Hs$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:geom}
\norm{T^n(\Ss_0)-\Sstar}\;\le\;\rho^{\,n}\,\norm{\Ss_0-\Sstar}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $\Ss_1,\Ss_2\in\Hs$,
$T(\Ss_1)-T(\Ss_2)=(\Ss_1-\Ss_2)+h\,[\mathbf F_1-\mathbf F_2]$ where
$\mathbf F_j=\mathbf F(\Ss_j,A(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss_j)))$. Expanding the
squared norm,
\[
\norm{T(\Ss_1)-T(\Ss_2)}^2=\norm{\Ss_1-\Ss_2}^2
+2h(\Ss_1-\Ss_2)^{\top}(\mathbf F_1-\mathbf F_2)
+h^2\norm{\mathbf F_1-\mathbf F_2}^2 .
\]
The Lyapunov descent condition applied to the pair gives
$(\Ss_1-\Ss_2)^{\top}(\mathbf F_1-\mathbf F_2)\le-\alpha\norm{\Ss_1-\Ss_2}^2$,
and the Lipschitz bound gives
$\norm{\mathbf F_1-\mathbf F_2}^2\le L_F^2\norm{\Ss_1-\Ss_2}^2$. Hence
$\norm{T(\Ss_1)-T(\Ss_2)}^2\le(1-2h\alpha+h^2L_F^2)\norm{\Ss_1-\Ss_2}^2$.
For $h<2\alpha/L_F^2$ the factor is $<1$, so $\rho<1$. The Banach theorem
then yields the unique fixed point and the geometric
bound~\citep{kreyszig1989functional}. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Reading the rate]
\label{rem:rate}
$\rho$ is the per-step error-contraction factor: after $n$ steps the
distance to setpoint is at most $\rho^n$ of the initial distance. The
bound \eqref{eq:step} is the familiar stability limit on an explicit
integrator step; choosing $h$ well below $2\alpha/L_F^2$ gives fast
convergence, choosing it near the limit gives slow but still monotone
convergence. The rate is set entirely by the plant and the gain, not by
anything in the monitoring layer.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The sampling (Nyquist-type) cell-width bound}

The cell width must be small enough to resolve the fastest closed-loop
dynamics, and---by the floor---no smaller than $\floor$. This is a
spatial analogue of the Nyquist--Shannon sampling
criterion~\citep{shannon1948mathematical,astrom2008feedback}.

\begin{theorem}[Cell-width bound]
\label{thm:nyquist}
Let $\Bproc$ be the closed-loop process bandwidth, i.e.\ the magnitude of
the largest imaginary part of any closed-loop pole of the linearised
dynamics, expressed per sampling interval. For the cell partition to
represent the process without aliasing, the cell width must satisfy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cellbound}
\floor\;\le\;\wcell\;\le\;\frac{1}{2\Bproc}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently the sampling rate must obey $\fref=1/h\ge2\Bproc$. A
partition is \emph{admissible} iff \eqref{eq:cellbound} holds, which
requires $\floor\le 1/(2\Bproc)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Sampling the S-state at rate $\fref$ recovers spectral content up to
$\fref/2$ without aliasing; the closed-loop signal has content up to
$\Bproc$, so faithful reconstruction requires $\fref\ge2\Bproc$, i.e.\
$h\le1/(2\Bproc)$~\citep{shannon1948mathematical,astrom2008feedback}. In
the state space, the spatial resolution of the quantiser is the cell
width; the finest spatial frequency it represents is $1/(2\wcell)$, so
faithful representation of a process with bandwidth $\Bproc$ requires
$\wcell\le1/(2\Bproc)$. The lower bound $\wcell\ge\floor$ is
\Cref{prop:floor}. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Feasibility]
\label{cor:feasible}
An admissible partition exists iff the resolution floor is fine enough
for the process bandwidth, $\floor\le1/(2\Bproc)$. If
$\floor>1/(2\Bproc)$ the instruments cannot resolve the process fast
enough and no cell-partition controller (indeed no sampled-data
controller at rate $\fref$) can regulate it without aliasing; the remedy
is a faster or finer instrument, not a finer grid.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from the two-sided bound \eqref{eq:cellbound}: the interval
$[\floor,1/(2\Bproc)]$ is non-empty iff $\floor\le1/(2\Bproc)$. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{cor:feasible} is a genuine design constraint on a reactor: it tells
the engineer, before commissioning, whether the chosen DO/pH/temperature
probes are fast enough for the culture's dynamics, and turns a vague
question of ``is our sensing good enough'' into a checkable inequality.

\subsection{The control layer as a finite-volume fluid-dynamics solver}
\label{sec:fvm}

The three results just proved---a cell partition of a bounded field, an
explicit time-step operator, a Courant-type bound relating the step to the
cell size---are not merely reminiscent of computational fluid dynamics;
they have the same structure as an explicit finite-volume
integrator~\citep{astrom2008feedback}. This is not a coincidence. The
bioreactor broth \emph{is} a fluid, and the quantities the controller
regulates (dissolved oxygen, temperature, pH) are transported fields on
that fluid; a controller that partitions their state space into cells and
steps a conservation-respecting update is, structurally, solving a
transport problem on that field. We make the correspondence precise as a
mapping and then bound its scope.

\caption{Structural correspondence between an explicit finite-volume

\label{tab:fvm}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Finite-volume solver} & \textbf{Cell-partition control layer} \\
\midrule

Control volume (cell) & Partition cell $C_i$ (\Cref{def:cells}) \\
Cell-averaged field value & Cell centroid $\bar\Ss_i$ (\Cref{def:centroid}) \\
Explicit time-step operator & $T(\Ss)=\Ss+h\mathbf F(\cdot)$ (\Cref{eq:discop}) \\
Update rate $\to$ steady state & Banach contraction $\rho^n$ (\Cref{thm:banach}) \\
CFL stability condition & step bound $h<2\alpha/L_F^2$ (\Cref{eq:step}) \\

Diffusive neighbour coupling & Lipschitz coupling $L_F$; gain $K$ \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{proposition}[Finite-volume structure of the control step]
\label{prop:fvm}
The discrete-time control operator $T$ of \eqref{eq:discop} is an explicit
one-step finite-volume-type integrator of the field dynamics
\eqref{eq:plant} on the cell partition $\Cs$, in the following precise
sense:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item it advances a cell-averaged state (the occupied cell, reconstructed
to its centroid, \Cref{def:centroid}) by an explicit Euler step of the
field equation \eqref{eq:plant};
\item its convergence to the steady field $\Sstar$ is geometric with rate
$\rho$ (\Cref{thm:banach}), the discrete analogue of relaxation to
equilibrium;
\item its stability is governed by a Courant--Friedrichs--Lewy-type bound
\eqref{eq:step} on the step $h$ relative to the field's Lipschitz rate
$L_F$, and by a grid-resolution bound \eqref{eq:cellbound} on the cell
width relative to the process bandwidth $\Bproc$---the two conditions an
explicit finite-volume scheme must satisfy to be stable and non-aliasing.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
(i) By \eqref{eq:discop}, $T(\Ss)=\Ss+h\mathbf F(\Ss,A(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss)))$
is the forward-Euler step of \eqref{eq:plant}; the action is constant on
each cell (\Cref{def:cellaction}), so within a cell the update uses the
single cell-representative value, exactly a finite-volume cell average.
(ii) is \Cref{thm:banach}. (iii) The step bound \eqref{eq:step} is the
contraction condition of \Cref{thm:banach}; written as
$h<2\alpha/L_F^2$ it bounds the explicit step by the field's local rate,
the control-theoretic form of a CFL condition, and \eqref{eq:cellbound} is
the grid-resolution bound of \Cref{thm:nyquist}. All three restate results
already proved; the proposition only names their finite-volume reading.
\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Scope of the fluid-dynamics reading]
\label{rem:fvm-scope}
We claim a \emph{structural correspondence}, not an identity. The control
layer is a finite-volume-type integrator of a bounded transported field;
it is \emph{not} a discretisation of the incompressible Navier--Stokes
equations, and we make no such claim: it carries no explicit
momentum-continuity coupling, no pressure-projection step, and no
divergence-free constraint on a velocity field. What it shares with a
computational-fluid-dynamics solver is the load-bearing structure---control
volumes, an explicit conservative time-step, a CFL-type stability bound, a
grid-resolution bound, and geometric relaxation to a steady field---and
this shared structure is exactly why the stability theory of
\Cref{sec:stability} reads as the convergence theory of an explicit field
solver. The correspondence is offered as orientation and as a bridge to the
computational-physics literature; no theorem in this paper depends on it,
and every stability guarantee stands on the control-theoretic proofs
alone. A reader who rejects the fluid reading entirely loses nothing but
the analogy.
\end{remark}

The value of \Cref{prop:fvm} is conceptual and practical at once.
Conceptually, it explains why a controller for a fluid process should look
like a field solver: regulating transported quantities on a stirred fluid
\emph{is} a transport problem, and the cell-partition controller is the
finite-volume method for it. Practically, it imports the mature
intuition of explicit solvers---refine the grid and you must shorten the
step (\Cref{thm:nyquist,thm:banach} jointly); the Courant number is the
ratio the engineer tunes---directly into control commissioning.

\section{Structural incorruptibility}
\label{sec:security}
% =====================================================================

A networked bioreactor controller is a cyber-physical system, and the
integrity of a pharmaceutical process depends on the controller doing
only what it was verified to do~\citep{fda2004pat,cardenas2008secure}.
The cell-partition control layer has a strong integrity property that is
architectural, not cryptographic: because the runtime dispatch depends
only on cell membership and the action set is fixed at compile time, no
sensor value---however corrupted---can drive the plant to an action
outside the pre-approved finite set. We make this precise and then bound
the effect of the residual attacks that remain.

\subsection{No path from payload to unapproved action}

\begin{theorem}[Structural incorruptibility]
\label{thm:incorr}
Let the closed-loop S-dynamics under a network perturbation
$\mathbf d_{\mathrm{net}}$ be
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pert}
\dot{\Ss}\;=\;\mathbf F\bigl(\Ss,\,\mathbf u\bigr)\;+\;\mathbf B_d\,\mathbf d_{\mathrm{net}} .
\end{equation}
If the controller computes its action solely as
$\mathbf u=A(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss))$ from the S-state, with $A$ a fixed
finite map compiled offline, then no content carried by any sensor packet
can change $\mathbf u$ except by moving $\Ss$ across a cell boundary, and
every reachable action lies in the pre-approved set $\Us$. In particular
the direct payload-to-plant gain is zero: $\mathbf B_d=\mathbf 0$ for any
perturbation acting through packet content rather than through the
physical channels.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The controller is the composition of two maps: a transducer
$\mathrm{ext}$ that reads the physical channels and returns the S-state
$\Ss$, discarding all non-physical packet fields; and the fixed
cell-action map $A\circ\mathrm{cell}$. Thus
$\mathbf u=A(\mathrm{cell}(\mathrm{ext}(\text{packet})))$ depends on the
packet only through the physical S-state it induces. Any
$\mathbf d_{\mathrm{net}}$ that acts on packet payload fields enters only
fields that $\mathrm{ext}$ discards; it therefore has no influence on
$\Ss$ and hence none on $\mathbf u$, giving $\mathbf B_d=\mathbf 0$ for
the payload channel. The codomain of $A$ is the finite pre-approved set
$\Us$; no input produces an action outside $\Us$, because $A$ is a
lookup into $\Us$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Physical grounding]
\label{cor:grounding}
The only way to induce a false control action is to change the physical
S-state the transducers actually observe---i.e.\ to manipulate the
physical process or the transducers themselves. An adversary confined to
the network layer cannot inject a state the transducers do not measure.
This is the observability-separation
principle~\citep{luenberger1979dynamic} in a security reading: control
authority is grounded in physical measurement, not in message content.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{thm:incorr}, $\mathbf u$ is a function of $\Ss$ alone, and $\Ss$
is produced by $\mathrm{ext}$ from the physical transducers. To change
$\mathbf u$ one must change $\Ss$, which requires changing what the
transducers measure. A network-only adversary alters packet content,
which $\mathrm{ext}$ discards. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Bounded effect of the residual attacks}

Three manipulations remain available to a network adversary; each has a
bounded, benign effect.

\begin{proposition}[Bounded spoofing]
\label{prop:spoof}
An injected or delayed measurement shifts $\Ss$ by at most one cell along
each affected channel, so the resulting action is $A$ of an adjacent
cell---still a pre-approved action in $\Us$. No arbitrary action is
reachable.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A single spurious or delayed sample perturbs a channel's S-value; because
cell boundaries are separated by at least $\wcell\ge\floor$
(\Cref{prop:floor}), a bounded perturbation moves the state at most to an
adjacent cell. The action of any cell is in $\Us$ by \Cref{def:cellaction}.
\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Replay resistance]
\label{prop:replay}
A replayed old measurement is rejected by the monotone committed record:
each accepted sample must carry a record index exceeding the last, so a
stale sample with an old index is discarded without cryptographic
overhead.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{prop:monotone} the record $M$ is strictly increasing. Tagging
each accepted sample with the current $M$ and rejecting any sample whose
tag is $\le$ the last accepted tag rejects any replay of an earlier
sample, since its tag is strictly smaller. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Denial of service is detectable]
\label{prop:dos}
Suppressing measurements is a denial-of-service attack, not an integrity
attack; it is detected by the monitoring layer as loss of confirmation
and triggers the decline-to-safe-state path of
\Cref{sec:monitoring,sec:separation}, which itself requires no trusted
network input.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Absence of expected samples reduces ensemble coherence
(\Cref{sec:federation}); \Cref{alg:decohere} detects the drop and invokes
the safe-state fallback, which is a control-layer action from $\Us$
(\Cref{sec:separation}). No integrity violation of the plant occurs. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why austerity buys integrity]
\label{rem:austerity}
The incorruptibility of \Cref{thm:incorr} is a direct consequence of the
control layer's austerity: it consumes only a cell index and dispatches
from a fixed table. A controller that instead ran a rich state estimator
over fused sensor data, or that parsed structured sensor payloads, would
expose exactly the payload-to-action path that \Cref{thm:incorr}
eliminates. This is the security argument for placing all richness in the
downstream monitoring layer, behind a one-directional interface, rather
than in the control law---which is what \Cref{sec:separation} formalises.
This structural argument complements, and does not replace, standard
network hardening~\citep{teixeira2015secure}.
\end{remark}

\section{The monitoring layer: a confirmation calculus}
\label{sec:monitoring}
% =====================================================================

The control layer regulates; the monitoring layer \emph{confirms}. Its
job is to decide, from possibly-redundant sensing, whether the process
has entered a region of interest---an excursion, a phase transition of
the culture, an alarm condition---and to do so honestly, reporting
disagreement rather than papering over it. The layer produces a
confirmation status and, at most, a request to the control layer to
change setpoint or enter a safe mode; it never computes a control action.
Everything in this section is downstream of \Cref{sec:control} and, by
\Cref{sec:separation}, additive to it.

\subsection{Confirmation as cell-crossing; the CUSUM identity}

The natural unit of confirmation is a cell-boundary crossing: the S-state
leaving the cell it was regulated into is exactly the event a monitor
should flag. We show this crossing coincides with the classical CUSUM
out-of-control alarm~\citep{page1954continuous,montgomery2020spc}, so the
monitoring layer inherits a mature detection theory with a known
false-alarm rate.

\begin{definition}[Channel deviation]
\label{def:deviation}
For a single channel, let $\DP(k)$ be the deviation of the $k$-th sample
from the channel's setpoint value (in S-coordinates), with reference
level $\mu_0=0$ at the setpoint.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[CUSUM--cell-crossing identity]
\label{thm:cusum}
Consider the two-sided cumulative-sum statistic
\begin{align*}
C_k^{+}&=\max\!\bigl(0,\;C_{k-1}^{+}+\DP(k)-\mu_0\bigr),\\
C_k^{-}&=\min\!\bigl(0,\;C_{k-1}^{-}+\DP(k)+\mu_0\bigr),
\end{align*}
with $C_0^{\pm}=0$ and control limit $H=\wcell/2$ (half a cell width).
Then the CUSUM out-of-control event is exactly the event that the running
sum leaves the cell $[-H,H]$ centred on the setpoint:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cusum}
\{C_k^{+}>H\}\cup\{C_k^{-}<-H\}
\;\equiv\;
\Bigl\{\,\Bigl|\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{k}\DP(i)\Bigr|>H\,\Bigr\}.
\end{equation}
That is, a cell-boundary crossing is a CUSUM alarm and conversely.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
With $\mu_0=0$, the recursion accumulates the centred deviations, so
$C_k^{+}$ tracks the positive cumulative drift and $C_k^{-}$ the negative
cumulative drift of $\sum_i\DP(i)$. The cell membership predicate for a
scalar channel centred at the setpoint is
$\bigl|\sum_{i\le k}\DP(i)\bigr|\le H=\wcell/2$. The alarm
$C_k^{+}>H$ or $C_k^{-}<-H$ triggers exactly when the cumulative drift
exceeds $H$ in magnitude, i.e.\ when the state leaves $[-H,H]$; the two
conditions coincide term by term. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Tunable false-alarm rate]
\label{cor:arl}
The in-control average run length of the monitor is a known increasing
function of the cell half-width $H=\wcell/2$~\citep{lucas1982fast}:
widening cells reduces false alarms and slows detection; narrowing cells
(down to the floor $\floor$) speeds detection and raises false alarms.
The cell geometry chosen for control (\Cref{thm:nyquist}) thus \emph{also}
fixes the monitor's statistical operating point, with no separate tuning.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The CUSUM average run length is a standard increasing function of the
decision limit for a given noise level~\citep{lucas1982fast,montgomery2020spc};
by \Cref{thm:cusum} the limit is $H=\wcell/2$. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Composing independent confirmations}

A single probe confirming an excursion may be wrong; independent probes
compound. We record the composition law and its consequence.

\begin{definition}[Confirmation power]
\label{def:power}
A confirmation source $\gamma$ (a probe, a soft sensor, an inference)
applied to a candidate excursion has \emph{power}
$\kappa(\gamma)\in[0,1]$: the fraction of the residual doubt it removes,
$\kappa=1$ meaning it settles the question and $\kappa=0$ meaning it is
uninformative.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Multiplicative composition]
\label{thm:multiplicative}
For independent confirmation sources $\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_n$ with powers
$\kappa_1,\dots,\kappa_n$, the combined confirmation power is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mult}
\kappa(\gamma_1\circ\cdots\circ\gamma_n)\;=\;1-\prod_{i=1}^{n}(1-\kappa_i).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $r_0=1$ be the initial residual doubt. Applying $\gamma_1$ leaves
residual $r_1=(1-\kappa_1)r_0$ by \Cref{def:power}; independence means
$\gamma_2$ removes the same fraction of what remains,
$r_2=(1-\kappa_2)r_1$; by induction $r_n=\prod_i(1-\kappa_i)$. The
combined power is $1-r_n$, which is \eqref{eq:mult}. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Diversify, do not repeat]
\label{cor:diversify}
Repeating one source of power $\kappa$ a total of $n$ times yields
combined power $1-(1-\kappa)^n$, strictly increasing but bounded away
from $1$ by the correlation of the repeats; $n$ \emph{independent}
sources of comparable power reach high confirmation far faster. A monitor
should therefore prefer diverse evidentiary channels (an amperometric DO
probe, an optical DO probe, and an off-gas $\mathrm{O_2}$ balance) over
repeated reads of one probe.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from \eqref{eq:mult} with equal $\kappa_i=\kappa$ for the
repeated case; independence is what makes the product collapse fastest.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Coherence: robust grounding needs three}

An excursion that will trigger a setpoint change or a safe-state entry
should not rest on a single source that could itself be faulty. We show
robustness to any one source failing requires at least three mutually
supporting sources.

\begin{definition}[Support and coherence]
\label{def:support}
Source $\gamma_j$ \emph{supports} source $\gamma_i$ for a candidate
excursion if the presence of $\gamma_j$'s evidence reinforces (does not
contradict) $\gamma_i$'s. The \emph{support graph} has an edge
$j\to i$ when $\gamma_j$ supports $\gamma_i$. A confirmation is
\emph{coherent} if its support graph contains a directed cycle of length
$\ge3$ whose members mutually support one another.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Coherence requires three]
\label{thm:triangle}
A confirmation is robust to the failure or dissent of any single source
if and only if its support graph contains a mutually supporting cycle of
length at least three. A single source is unfounded under its own
failure; two mutually citing sources collapse when either is removed;
three mutually supporting sources survive the removal of any one.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{(Necessity.)} If the support graph is acyclic it has a source node
with no incoming support; removing it (or any node all of whose support
is downstream) leaves a successor unsupported, and the confirmation
collapses. A $1$-cycle is a source citing only itself, vacuous. A
$2$-cycle is two sources citing only each other; removing either leaves
the other unsupported. Hence robustness requires a cycle of length
$\ge3$. \emph{(Sufficiency.)} In a mutually supporting triangle each
source is supported by the other two; removing any one leaves the
remaining two still supporting each other directly, so the confirmation
persists. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Sign-only decidability]
\label{cor:signonly}
Whether a confirmation is coherent is decidable from the \emph{signs} of
pairwise support (does each pair reinforce or contradict) alone, without
any confirmation-power magnitudes, since \Cref{thm:triangle} depends only
on the cycle structure of the support graph.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
\Cref{thm:triangle} is stated entirely in terms of directed-cycle
existence in the sign-defined support graph, independent of the numerical
$\kappa_i$. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Two honest endings: confirm or decline}

\begin{definition}[Closure of a confirmation]
\label{def:closure}
A confirmation is \emph{closed} at a given stage if no further available
source, when consulted, would move the excursion into a materially
different equivalence class of outcomes than those already reached. This
is strictly stronger than ``confidence exceeded a threshold'': it asks
that the space of available evidence has stopped producing new
conclusions.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Confirm-or-decline dichotomy]
\label{thm:decline}
Every confirmation over a finite set of available sources terminates in
exactly one of two states:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Confirmed}: the reached outcomes collapse to a single
equivalence class; the monitor reports that class (e.g.\ ``DO excursion
below setpoint, confirmed'').
\item \textbf{Declined}: the reached outcomes stabilise but contain more
than one class (independent sources irreducibly disagree); the monitor
reports \emph{decline}, naming the distinct classes, and---for a
safety-relevant excursion---requests the control layer's safe state.
\end{enumerate}
No confirmation over a finite source set runs forever.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The source set is finite, so the set of reachable outcome classes is
finite and can only grow finitely often before every source has been
consulted against every reached class; at that point \Cref{def:closure}
is satisfied by exhaustion. If the stabilised class set is a singleton,
case~(i); if it has more than one class, no single outcome is licensed
and the monitor declines, case~(ii). The two are exhaustive and mutually
exclusive. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Decline is a first-class output]
\label{rem:decline}
\Cref{thm:decline}(ii) is the honesty guarantee the bioprocess setting
needs: when the amperometric and optical DO probes disagree beyond
reconciliation, the correct monitor output is not to silently pick one
but to declare disagreement and fall back. Because the fallback is a
control-layer safe action (\Cref{sec:separation}), declining is safe by
construction, and it never requires the monitoring layer to have been
correct.
\end{remark}

\section{Sensor federation}
\label{sec:federation}
% =====================================================================

The confirmation calculus of \Cref{sec:monitoring} composes independent
sources but does not say how a heterogeneous, changing set of probes
agrees cheaply. This section gives the mechanism: sensors federate as
coupled oscillators, agreeing by phase entrainment rather than by
exchanging content. The federation makes each measurement a member of a
larger ensemble whose collective confirmation is, at coherence, free; it
degrades gracefully as sensors drop out; and it admits new sensors only
when they pay their way in information. It lives entirely in the
monitoring layer.

\subsection{The temporal-agent model}

\begin{definition}[Temporal sensor agent]
\label{def:agent}
A \emph{temporal sensor agent} is a tuple $(f_i,\phi_i,\Cs_i)$ where
$f_i>0$ is the natural frequency of a reference oscillator carried with
sensor $i$, $\phi_i(t)\in[0,2\pi)$ is its phase, and $\Cs_i$ is the
sensor's local cell registry (the same partition of \Cref{sec:foundations}
in the phase view). Sensor $i$'s measurement enters as a frequency
perturbation of its oscillator: if the measured quantity shifts the
channel by $\delta\omega_i$, the inter-pulse interval becomes
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dp-osc}
\DP_i(k)\;=\;\frac{2\pi}{\omega_i+\delta\omega_i(k)},\qquad\omega_i=2\pi f_i,
\end{equation}
so a change in the physical variable shifts $\DP_i$ into a different cell
without any explicit encoding step.
\end{definition}

Equation \eqref{eq:dp-osc} is the join operation: to place a measurement
into the ensemble is simply to let it perturb the sensor's oscillator.
The ensemble then coordinates through the coupling of these oscillators.

\subsection{Coherence and critical coupling}

\begin{definition}[Ensemble order parameter]
\label{def:order}
For $n$ sensor agents the \emph{ensemble order parameter} is the complex
average
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:order}
\Rens(t)\,e^{i\psi(t)}\;=\;\frac1n\sum_{j=1}^{n}e^{i\phi_j(t)},
\qquad\Rens\in[0,1].
\end{equation}
$\Rens=0$ is complete incoherence (phases uniform), $\Rens=1$ perfect
phase lock. $\Rens$ is the monitoring layer's measure of how well the
sensor ensemble agrees.
\end{definition}

The agents couple through the Kuramoto
dynamics~\citep{kuramoto1984chemical,strogatz2000kuramoto}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kuramoto}
\dot\phi_i=\omega_i+\frac{K}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\sin(\phi_j-\phi_i),
\end{equation}
with coupling strength $K\ge0$ set by the shared timing infrastructure.

\begin{theorem}[Critical coupling]
\label{thm:critical}
Let the natural frequencies $\{\omega_i\}$ be drawn from a symmetric
unimodal distribution with standard deviation $\sigma_\omega$. In the
large-ensemble limit there is a critical coupling
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kc}
\Kc\;=\;\frac{2\sigma_\omega}{\pi}
\end{equation}
such that for $K<\Kc$ the only stable state is incoherence
($\Rens^{\ast}=0$), and for $K>\Kc$ a coherent state emerges with
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rstar}
\Rens^{\ast}=\sqrt{1-\Kc/K}
\end{equation}
via a supercritical pitchfork bifurcation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is the standard Kuramoto mean-field result. In the large-$n$ limit
each oscillator feels the mean field $KR$; oscillators with
$\abs{\omega_i}\le KR^{\ast}$ lock, the rest drift and contribute no net
coherence. The self-consistency condition
$1=K\!\int_{-KR^{\ast}}^{KR^{\ast}}\!\sqrt{1-\omega^2/(KR^{\ast})^2}\,g(\omega)\,\mathrm d\omega$,
expanded near the bifurcation, gives $\Kc=2/(\pi g(0))=2\sigma_\omega/\pi$
for the reference symmetric-unimodal case, and the pitchfork normal form
gives \eqref{eq:rstar}; see \citet{strogatz2000kuramoto,acebron2005kuramoto}
for the full derivation. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Reading $\Kc$ for a reactor]
\label{rem:kc}
Equation \eqref{eq:kc} says the ensemble locks when the shared timing
coupling exceeds a threshold set by the spread of the sensors' oscillator
frequencies. Probes of the same make and batch have small
$\sigma_\omega$, so modest coupling locks them; a heterogeneous mix of
probe types has larger $\sigma_\omega$ and needs stronger coupling. This
is a design lever: matching probes, or increasing the timing-pulse rate,
raises coherence.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Free collective confirmation at phase lock}

\begin{theorem}[Zero-overhead confirmation]
\label{thm:zero}
Suppose the ensemble is phase-locked, $\Rens\ge0.95$, and the cell
partition has width $\wcell>2\pi(1-\Rens)/\bar\omega$ where
$\bar\omega$ is the mean frequency. Then all agents assign the same cell
to the same event without exchanging any confirmation messages: the
per-event coordination cost of the ensemble is zero.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At phase lock all agents rotate at the common mean rate up to a residual
phase error bounded by $\arccos(\Rens)$. The induced spread in
inter-pulse interval is
$\abs{\DP_i-\DP_j}\le2\pi(1-\Rens)/\bar\omega=:\varepsilon_0$. If
$\wcell>\varepsilon_0$, two agents' $\DP$ values for the same event differ
by less than a cell width and hence lie in the same cell. Each agent
therefore computes the same cell assignment locally from its own reading;
no message is needed to reconcile them, so the coordination cost is zero.
\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Phase lock is free redundancy]
\label{rem:zero}
\Cref{thm:zero} is the payoff of federation: when the sensor ensemble is
coherent, every probe independently and simultaneously confirms the same
cell, so the excursion is confirmed with the full multiplicative power of
\Cref{thm:multiplicative} at no communication cost. A reactor with three
coherent, phase-locked DO-related channels confirms a DO excursion three
ways for free---which is exactly the coherence-triangle robustness of
\Cref{thm:triangle} realised at no overhead.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Admission: earn your place in the ensemble}

\begin{definition}[Marginal information and cost]
\label{def:admission}
For an ensemble whose joint confirmation state has Shannon entropy
$H_{\mathrm{fed}}$~\citep{shannon1948mathematical,cover2006elements}, the
\emph{marginal information} of a candidate sensor $i$ is
$\Delta H_i=H_{\mathrm{fed}}-H_{\mathrm{fed},-i}$, the entropy it adds;
its \emph{cost} $c_i$ is the timing-channel bandwidth it consumes to
remain phase-coherent.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Admission criterion]
\label{thm:admit}
Under the objective of maximising ensemble confirmation resolution per
unit timing-bandwidth, sensor $i$ should be admitted iff
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:admit}
\Delta H_i\;>\;c_i .
\end{equation}
The greedy rule \eqref{eq:admit} is $(1-1/e)$-optimal because the
ensemble entropy is a submodular function of the sensor set.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Maximising $H_{\mathrm{fed}}$ subject to a total-bandwidth budget is a
knapsack over sensors; because each added sensor's marginal contribution
$\Delta H_i$ is non-increasing in the current ensemble size (submodularity
of entropy under the independence model), the greedy benefit-over-cost
rule $\Delta H_i>c_i$ attains a $(1-1/e)$ approximation of the constrained
optimum~\citep{cover2006elements}. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{thm:admit} governs how the ensemble grows: a redundant probe that
adds little information is refused, a probe that opens a genuinely new
evidentiary channel is admitted, and---because admission is by marginal
information---adding independent channels sharpens the collective
confirmation rather than diluting it.

\subsection{Graceful decoherence: the ensemble degrades, it does not fail}

\begin{theorem}[Graceful degradation]
\label{thm:decohere}
If coupling falls below $\Kc$ (probes fail, timing degrades), the ensemble
does not collapse discontinuously: $\Rens$ relaxes downward and the
monitoring layer transitions to a higher-cost coordination regime,
maintaining confirmation at reduced resolution. In the limit
$\Rens\to0$, each sensor confirms only from its own reading---a set of
independent single-channel monitors. A single sensor loss reduces $\Rens$
by at most $\Rens/n$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $K<\Kc$ the coherent fixed point of \eqref{eq:rstar} disappears and
$\Rens$ relaxes toward $0$ continuously at rate set by the curvature of
the Kuramoto potential at $R=0$; there is no jump. Removing one of $n$
equal-weight terms from the average \eqref{eq:order} changes $\Rens$ by at
most $1/n$ of its value. As $\Rens\to0$ the coupling term in
\eqref{eq:kuramoto} vanishes and each agent's cell assignment depends
only on its own $\DP_i$. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{thm:decohere} is the monitoring-side statement of the worst case:
however the ensemble decoheres, it decays to a collection of independent
per-channel monitors, and each of those still feeds---through the same
one-directional interface---an ordinary cell-partition controller. The
separation theorem (\Cref{sec:separation}) proves that this worst case
leaves the control-layer guarantees intact.

\section{Virtual sensors: two concurrent scripts}
\label{sec:virtual}
% =====================================================================

The confirmation calculus and the federation treat a measurement as a
member of an ensemble. We now use the identity \emph{observation =
computation}---that a sensor reading and a computed value are the same
kind of object, a value that lands in a cell---to add a capability that no
purely physical sensing possesses: \emph{virtual sensors}, channels that
have no physical probe and exist only in software, synthesised at a node
from other channels. Because a virtual sensor is the same kind of object
as a physical one, it joins the state hypercube, the cell partition, and
(where useful) the federated ensemble on exactly equal footing.

\subsection{Observation and computation are the same operation}

\begin{principle}[Observation--computation equivalence]
\label{prin:obs-comp}
To \emph{observe} a channel is to place its current value into a cell of
the partition; to \emph{compute} a channel is to place a
function of other channels into a cell of the partition. Both operations
have the same type---a map into $\Cs$---and both produce the same kind of
output: a cell membership. Nothing in the control law
\eqref{eq:controllaw}, the stability certificate (\Cref{cor:lmi}), or the
confirmation calculus (\Cref{sec:monitoring}) distinguishes a coordinate
that was measured from one that was computed; each is simply a coordinate
of $\Ss$ with a cell.
\end{principle}

\Cref{prin:obs-comp} is what licenses running two scripts side by side. A
\emph{sensor script} reads the physical probes and produces their cell
memberships. A \emph{metabolic script} runs concurrently and, at
designated nodes, computes new coordinates from the physical ones; its
outputs are cell memberships of the same type. The two scripts write into
the same S-state; the controller and monitor cannot tell, and need not
tell, which coordinates came from which script.

\subsection{The virtual channel}

\begin{definition}[Virtual sensor]
\label{def:virtual}
A \emph{virtual sensor} is a channel $v$ whose S-value is not measured but
computed at a node by a fixed, offline-verified map
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:virtual}
S_v \;=\; g_v\!\bigl(S_{i_1},\dots,S_{i_r}\bigr)\in[0,1],
\end{equation}
where $g_v\colon[0,1]^r\to[0,1]$ is Lipschitz and $S_{i_1},\dots,S_{i_r}$
are the (physical or virtual) input channels feeding the node. The virtual
channel carries the same S-coordinate, cell, and centroid structure as a
physical channel (\Cref{sec:foundations}), and appears as an additional
axis of the state hypercube, extending $\Hs$ from $[0,1]^m$ to
$[0,1]^{m+1}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[The sensor that exists only in the program]
\label{rem:only-in-program}
The point of \eqref{eq:virtual} is that $g_v$ may compute a quantity for
which no instrument exists. From pH, off-gas $\mathrm{CO_2}$, and
temperature at one node, a metabolic script can synthesise a
respiratory/metabolic-state channel---a value that no single probe
measures but that the operator actually cares about
(\Cref{sec:bioreactor}). The virtual channel is a genuine coordinate of
the state: the controller can regulate it, and the monitor can confirm
excursions in it, exactly as for a physical channel.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Safe-by-construction virtual channels}

Because a virtual channel may be load-bearing for control
(\Cref{sec:separation}), its computation must never destroy the stability
certificate when its inputs are unavailable or out of range. We require it
to degrade to a defined constant.

\begin{definition}[Fallback-guarded virtual sensor]
\label{def:fallback}
A virtual sensor is \emph{fallback-guarded} if it carries a validity
predicate $\mathrm{ok}_v$ (true iff all inputs are present and in range)
and a fixed \emph{fallback value} $S_v^{0}\in[0,1]$, and computes
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:guarded}
S_v \;=\;
\begin{cases}
g_v(S_{i_1},\dots,S_{i_r}) & \text{if }\mathrm{ok}_v,\\[2pt]
S_v^{0} & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The fallback $S_v^{0}$ is chosen to lie in a cell whose control action is
conservative and whose configuration is certified stable.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Virtual channels preserve the certificate]
\label{prop:virtual-stable}
If every virtual channel is fallback-guarded (\Cref{def:fallback}) and the
per-cell LMIs \eqref{eq:lmi} of \Cref{cor:lmi} are verified over the
\emph{extended} hypercube $[0,1]^{m+v}$ (physical plus virtual axes),
including all cells reachable when any virtual channel is at its fallback
$S_v^{0}$, then the control layer is globally asymptotically stable
whether or not the virtual computations are valid.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The extended state is $\tilde\Ss\in[0,1]^{m+v}$ with the virtual axes
appended. By \Cref{prin:obs-comp} the control law and certificate treat
$\tilde\Ss$ as an ordinary S-state, so \Cref{cor:lmi} applies to the
extended cells verbatim, giving global asymptotic stability
(\Cref{thm:pwlyap}) provided all reachable extended cells pass their LMIs.
When $\mathrm{ok}_v$ fails, \eqref{eq:guarded} pins $S_v$ to the constant
$S_v^{0}$, so the reachable set collapses onto the slice
$\{S_v=S_v^{0}\}$, whose cells are among those certified by hypothesis.
Hence the certificate holds in both the valid and the fallback regime.
\qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{prop:virtual-stable} is what makes a control-layer virtual sensor
safe: the LMIs are simply checked on the larger grid, and the guard
guarantees that a failed metabolic computation freezes the virtual
coordinate at a certified-safe value rather than injecting a wild one.

\subsection{Virtual channels as confirmation sources}

A virtual channel is also a confirmation source in the sense of
\Cref{sec:monitoring}: an excursion in the computed metabolic-state
channel is a cell-crossing (\Cref{thm:cusum}) and composes multiplicatively
with physical confirmations (\Cref{thm:multiplicative}). It can therefore
turn a fragile pair of physical probes into a robust coherence triangle
(\Cref{thm:triangle}): two physical DO probes plus one computed
metabolic-state channel is a three-source cycle.

\begin{remark}[Independence: the honest caveat]
\label{rem:virtual-independence}
We count a virtual channel as a confirmation source, and in the analysis
of \Cref{sec:bioreactor} we treat it as independent to obtain the
strongest composition. This is a modelling choice, and we flag its limit
plainly: a virtual channel computed from physical inputs
$S_{i_1},\dots,S_{i_r}$ shares any fault common to those inputs, so it is
\emph{not} independent of them against a correlated failure (a single
contamination shifting pH, $\mathrm{CO_2}$, and $T$ together also shifts
the virtual channel). Where robustness to correlated probe faults is
required, the coherence triangle (\Cref{thm:triangle}) must include at
least one leg---physical or virtual---that does not share inputs with the
others. The virtual channel adds a genuinely new confirmation only to the
extent that its map $g_v$ responds to a combination the individual probes
do not report on their own.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Ensemble invariants: information in the set, not the unit}
\label{sec:invariant}

The virtual sensor of \Cref{def:virtual} is still \emph{local}: it is a
function of a few named channels at one node, and it inherits any fault
common to those inputs (\Cref{rem:virtual-independence}). We now define a
stronger construct---a quantity that belongs to the sensor \emph{ensemble
as a whole}, is realised by no single unit, and is therefore
\emph{true for all units and present in none}. This is the computed
analogue of a physical conservation law: information carried by the
relations among the units rather than by any unit's reading.

Fix an ensemble of $n$ units (physical or virtual) reporting S-values
$\mathbf S^{\mathrm{ens}}=(S_1,\dots,S_n)$. Two units are
\emph{interchangeable} if they measure the same channel to the same
specification (e.g.\ two DO probes of the same type); substituting or
relabelling interchangeable units is a symmetry of the ensemble. Let
$G$ be the group of such relabellings (a subgroup of the symmetric group
$\mathfrak S_n$).

\begin{definition}[Ensemble invariant]
\label{def:ensemble-invariant}
A computed channel $S_\iota=\iota(\mathbf S^{\mathrm{ens}})$ is an
\emph{ensemble invariant} if it satisfies both:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Relabelling-invariance}: $\iota(\pi\cdot\mathbf
S^{\mathrm{ens}})=\iota(\mathbf S^{\mathrm{ens}})$ for every
$\pi\in G$---its value does not change when interchangeable units are
permuted or substituted; and
\item \textbf{Non-locality}: $\iota$ is not a function of any single
unit's reading alone; formally, for each $j$ there exist ensemble states
agreeing in $S_j$ but differing in $\iota$, so no unit determines $\iota$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Condition~(i) is what ``true for all units'' means precisely: the quantity
is defined on the ensemble in a way that references no particular unit's
identity, so it is a property the whole set possesses in common.
Condition~(ii) is what ``present in none'' means precisely: no single unit
carries it, because no single reading fixes it.

\begin{example}[The coherence order parameter is an ensemble invariant]
\label{ex:order-invariant}
The ensemble order parameter
$\Rens=\frac1n\bigl|\sum_j e^{i\phi_j}\bigr|$ of \Cref{def:order} is an
ensemble invariant: it is symmetric in the units (permuting the $\phi_j$
leaves the sum's magnitude unchanged), so it is relabelling-invariant; and
no single oscillator has a ``coherence''---$\Rens$ measures the agreement
\emph{between} units, so it is non-local. It is the canonical example of a
quantity true of the whole ensemble and present in no member.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Ensemble invariants are robust to any single-unit fault]
\label{prop:invariant-robust}
Let $S_\iota$ be an ensemble invariant with $\iota$ Lipschitz and
depending symmetrically on $n$ interchangeable units. Then corrupting,
losing, or spoofing any single unit $j$ perturbs $S_\iota$ by at most
$O(1/n)$; no single unit can move the invariant to an arbitrary value.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By relabelling-invariance $\iota$ is a symmetric function, so it depends
on unit $j$ only through $j$'s equal-weighted contribution among $n$. A
Lipschitz symmetric function changes by at most its Lipschitz constant
times the weight $1/n$ of one unit when that unit's value is altered
arbitrarily within $[0,1]$. Hence a single corrupted, absent, or spoofed
unit shifts $S_\iota$ by $O(1/n)$, vanishing as the ensemble grows. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{prop:invariant-robust} is the payoff of \Cref{def:ensemble-invariant}
and the reason the invariant is trustworthy where a single probe is not.
A physical probe reports \emph{its own} state and can be driven anywhere by
fouling it or spoofing it. An ensemble invariant reports a property of the
\emph{whole set} and cannot be moved by attacking any one unit, precisely
because no unit carries it. This is the coherence-triangle robustness
(\Cref{thm:triangle}) taken to its limit: the quantity that survives the
failure of \emph{any} single source is the one realised by \emph{none} of
them individually.

\begin{remark}[Two kinds of computed channel]
\label{rem:two-kinds}
The framework thus admits two computed channels of different character.
A \emph{local virtual sensor} (\Cref{def:virtual}) synthesises a
meaningful quantity---the respiratory state---from a few named channels;
it is actionable but shares its inputs' faults. An \emph{ensemble
invariant} (\Cref{def:ensemble-invariant}) synthesises a quantity that no
unit carries and every unit is consistent with; it is robust by
\Cref{prop:invariant-robust} but abstract. Both are computed channels of
the same type (\Cref{prin:obs-comp}), both may be fallback-guarded
(\Cref{def:fallback}) and regulated or confirmed against, and both live in
the extended hypercube on equal footing with physical channels. A reactor
uses the local virtual sensor to \emph{act} and the ensemble invariant to
\emph{trust}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Two scripts, one state}

\begin{proposition}[Concurrent scripts commute at the state]
\label{prop:concurrent}
The sensor script (writing physical coordinates) and the metabolic script
(writing virtual coordinates via \eqref{eq:guarded}) can execute
concurrently and their writes to distinct coordinates of $\tilde\Ss$
commute; the resulting extended state is well-defined provided each
virtual node's inputs are evaluated before its output (a topological order
on the node dependency graph, which is acyclic by construction).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Physical and virtual coordinates are distinct axes of $\tilde\Ss$, so
writes to them are to disjoint memory and commute. Within the metabolic
script, virtual nodes form a directed acyclic graph (a virtual channel
may feed another, never itself); any topological order evaluates every
node after its inputs, and all such orders yield the same $\tilde\Ss$
because each $g_v$ is a function. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{prop:concurrent} is the runtime statement of the two-script model:
the metabolic script is not a separate subsystem bolted on after sensing,
but a concurrent producer of state coordinates that the controller and
monitor consume identically to physical ones. In the bioreactor
(\Cref{sec:bioreactor}) this yields a metabolic-state coordinate the
reactor can regulate and confirm, assembled in software from probes that
individually report none of it.

\subsection{Sensor synthesis: switching between time and frequency}
\label{sec:synthesis}

We can now derive the operation that generates a new channel from
existing ones---the \emph{sensor synthesis map}. It is the concrete
mechanism behind ``a sensor that exists only in the program'': physical
channels enter, are switched into the frequency domain, combined there
under a conservation constraint, and switched back to a time-domain cell
the controller and monitor consume. The derivation rests on the
time--frequency bijection of \Cref{sec:switch} and the mean-recovery
constraint below, and needs no transform theory.

\subsubsection*{The mean-recovery constraint}

Synthesis combines the \emph{phases} of the input channels. The natural
combination that is symmetric in the inputs, and that inverts cleanly back
to a value, is the phase whose representation on the unit circle is the
mean of the inputs' representations.

\begin{definition}[Phase mean and mean-recovery]
\label{def:meanrec}
For input channels with S-values $S_1,\dots,S_r$ and phases
$\phi_j=\Phi(S_j)=2\pi S_j$ (\Cref{def:phase}), and nonnegative weights
$a_1,\dots,a_r$ with $\sum_j a_j=1$, the \emph{synthesised phase} is the
weighted circular mean
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:phasemean}
\phi_\star \;=\; \arg\!\Bigl(\textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{r} a_j\,e^{i\phi_j}\Bigr),
\qquad
\Rstar \;=\; \Bigl|\textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{r} a_j\,e^{i\phi_j}\Bigr|\in[0,1],
\end{equation}
and the \emph{synthesised value} is its time-domain image
$S_\star=\Phi^{-1}(\phi_\star)=\phi_\star/2\pi\in[0,1]$. The pair
$(\phi_\star,\Rstar)$ satisfies \emph{mean-recovery}: the resultant
$\sum_j a_j e^{i\phi_j}=\Rstar e^{i\phi_\star}$ recovers, in its
argument, the combined phase and, in its modulus, the concentration
$\Rstar$ of the inputs about that phase.
\end{definition}

Equation \eqref{eq:phasemean} is the switch put to work: the inputs are
carried to the frequency domain by $\Phi$, averaged as unit vectors
(mean-recovery), and the result is carried back to the time domain by
$\Phi^{-1}$. The modulus $\Rstar$ is exactly the ensemble order
parameter of \Cref{def:order} for the input phases, and is retained
because it is itself a useful synthesised channel (an ensemble invariant,
\Cref{prop:synth-invariant} below).

\begin{proposition}[Synthesis is a well-defined, Lipschitz virtual sensor]
\label{prop:synth-wd}
Whenever the resultant $\sum_j a_j e^{i\phi_j}\neq0$ \textup{(}equivalently
$\Rstar>0$\textup{)}, the synthesised value $S_\star$ of
\Cref{def:meanrec} is uniquely defined, and the map
$g_\star\colon(S_1,\dots,S_r)\mapsto S_\star$ is Lipschitz on any region
bounded away from $\Rstar=0$. Hence $g_\star$ is an admissible virtual
sensor \textup{(}\Cref{def:virtual}\textup{)}, and---equipped with the
fallback of \eqref{eq:guarded} triggered by $\Rstar$ below a floor---a
fallback-guarded one \textup{(}\Cref{def:fallback}\textup{)}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a nonzero planar vector the argument is unique modulo $2\pi$, so
$\phi_\star$ and hence $S_\star=\phi_\star/2\pi$ are well-defined when
$\Rstar>0$. The maps $\phi_j=2\pi S_j$, the weighted sum, and (away
from the origin) the argument are each smooth with bounded derivative on a
region with $\Rstar\ge\eta>0$; their composition is Lipschitz there.
Taking $g_v=g_\star$ in \Cref{def:virtual} makes $S_\star$ a virtual
sensor; declaring $\mathrm{ok}_v$ true iff $\Rstar\ge\eta$ and fixing
$S_v^{0}$ otherwise makes it fallback-guarded, with the degenerate
low-concentration case ($\Rstar<\eta$, inputs too dispersed to define
a mean direction) mapped to the certified-safe constant. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[The concentration is an ensemble invariant]
\label{prop:synth-invariant}
If the weights are equal, $a_j=1/r$, the concentration $\Rstar$ of
\eqref{eq:phasemean} is an ensemble invariant of the input units
\textup{(}\Cref{def:ensemble-invariant}\textup{)}: it is invariant under
permutation of the inputs and is realised by no single input. By
\Cref{prop:invariant-robust} a single corrupted input perturbs
$\Rstar$ by at most $O(1/r)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
With equal weights $\Rstar=\frac1r|\sum_j e^{i\phi_j}|$ is the
order-parameter modulus of \Cref{ex:order-invariant}, which is symmetric
in the $\phi_j$ (relabelling-invariant) and non-local (no single $\phi_j$
fixes it). It is therefore an ensemble invariant, and
\Cref{prop:invariant-robust} gives the $O(1/r)$ bound. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{prop:synth-invariant} is the precise sense in which synthesis
produces \emph{both} kinds of computed channel at once from the same
operation: the synthesised \emph{value} $S_\star$ (the argument) is a
local virtual sensor used to act, while the synthesised
\emph{concentration} $\Rstar$ (the modulus) is an ensemble invariant
used to trust---the value says \emph{where} the inputs agree, the
concentration says \emph{how much} they agree, and the latter is exactly
the correlated-fault detector of \Cref{rem:virtual-independence}.

\begin{theorem}[Sensor synthesis map]
\label{thm:synthesis}
Fix input channels $S_1,\dots,S_r$ and equal weights. The
\emph{synthesis map}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:synth}
(S_1,\dots,S_r)\ \xrightarrow{\ \Phi\ }\ (\phi_1,\dots,\phi_r)\
\xrightarrow{\ \eqref{eq:phasemean}\ }\
(\phi_\star,\Rstar)\ \xrightarrow{\ \Phi^{-1}\ }\ (S_\star,\Rstar)
\end{equation}
carries physical time-domain channels through the frequency domain and
back, producing two new channels---a value $S_\star$ and a concentration
$\Rstar$---each of which is a coordinate of the extended hypercube
with a cell (\Cref{prop:switch-cells}), consumable by the control layer
and the monitor identically to a physical channel. The map is invertible
in the sense that $\Phi,\Phi^{-1}$ lose no information
(\Cref{prop:switch}); only the combination step \eqref{eq:phasemean} is
many-to-one, exactly because it summarises $r$ inputs into a value and a
spread.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Each arrow of \eqref{eq:synth} is defined and justified above: $\Phi$ and
$\Phi^{-1}$ are the lossless bijections of \Cref{prop:switch}; the middle
arrow is \Cref{def:meanrec}, well-defined and Lipschitz by
\Cref{prop:synth-wd}. The outputs $S_\star,\Rstar\in[0,1]$ are
S-coordinates, hence carry cells by \Cref{prop:switch-cells}; by
\Cref{prin:obs-comp} the layers consume them as any coordinate. The
information statement is \Cref{prop:switch} (bijective ends) together with
the observation that averaging $r$ vectors is many-to-one. \qed
\end{proof}

\Cref{thm:synthesis} is the derivation the framework needs: it turns
``observation $=$ computation'' from a principle into an algorithm
(\Cref{alg:synth}). A measurement can be \emph{acquired} in either
domain---as a time-domain value or a frequency-domain phase---and
\emph{synthesised} with others by switching to the frequency domain,
averaging under mean-recovery, and switching back. The bioreactor's
respiratory-state channel (\Cref{sec:metabolic-script}) and its
DO-consistency invariant (\Cref{sec:bioreactor}) are both instances of
\eqref{eq:synth}: the same synthesis operation yields the actionable value
and the trustworthy invariant together.

\section{The separation theorem and fail-safe degradation}
\label{sec:separation}
% =====================================================================

We now prove the architectural claim that organises the whole system: the
monitoring layer is \emph{additive}. Its presence can only improve
confirmation and diagnostics; its complete failure reduces the system to
an ordinary provably-stable, structurally-incorruptible cell-partition
controller. The proof rests on a single structural fact---the interface
between the layers is one-directional---made precise below.

\subsection{The one-directional interface}

\begin{definition}[Layer interface]
\label{def:interface}
The \emph{interface} between the monitoring layer and the control layer
consists of exactly two admissible messages, each identical in kind to a
message a human operator could send:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Setpoint request}: propose a new setpoint
$\Sstar{}'\in\Hs$ from the finite set of pre-approved setpoints.
\item \textbf{Mode request}: request entry to a pre-approved mode, in
particular the \emph{safe mode} with its own pre-compiled cell-action map.
\end{enumerate}
No other channel exists from the monitoring layer to the control layer. In
particular, no monitoring output enters the control law \eqref{eq:controllaw}
as a term, a gain, a state estimate, or a parameter.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Interface admissibility]
\label{def:admissible-iface}
The interface is \emph{admissible} if every setpoint in the pre-approved
set and the safe mode each induce a cell-action map satisfying the
stability certificate of \Cref{cor:lmi} (its own set of per-cell LMIs
verified offline). Equivalently: whatever the monitoring layer requests,
the control layer switches only among pre-verified, individually stable
configurations.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Why this is the crux]
\label{rem:crux}
\Cref{def:interface} is the entire content of the separation. Because the
monitoring layer can only \emph{request} a switch among configurations
that are \emph{each} independently certified, and cannot inject anything
into the control computation, the control layer's correctness is a
property of its finite set of pre-verified configurations, not of any
monitoring output. The monitoring layer is, from the control layer's
point of view, indistinguishable from an operator---and an operator who
is silent, wrong, or absent cannot destabilise a controller that is
stable in every configuration it can be placed in.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Additivity and the worst-case theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Monitoring is additive]
\label{thm:additive}
Suppose the interface is admissible \textup{(}\Cref{def:admissible-iface}\textup{)}.
Then for any behaviour of the monitoring layer whatsoever---correct,
degraded, silent, or adversarial within the interface---the control layer
satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Stability}: in whatever configuration it currently holds,
the closed loop is globally asymptotically stable
(\Cref{thm:pwlyap,cor:lmi}) and geometrically convergent
(\Cref{thm:banach}).
\item \textbf{Incorruptibility}: no monitoring output causes an action
outside the pre-approved set $\Us$ (\Cref{thm:incorr}).
\item \textbf{Bounded switching transients}: each interface-induced
switch is between two individually stable configurations; under a
common Lyapunov function, or a dwell-time bound between switches, the
switched closed loop remains stable~\citep{liberzon2003switching}.
\end{enumerate}
No monitoring failure can violate \textup{(i)}--\textup{(iii)}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i) By \Cref{def:admissible-iface} every configuration the control layer
can occupy---any pre-approved setpoint, or the safe mode---has its own
verified per-cell LMIs, hence is globally asymptotically stable by
\Cref{cor:lmi} and geometrically convergent by \Cref{thm:banach}. The
monitoring layer selects only among these configurations
(\Cref{def:interface}), so whatever it does, the current configuration is
one of the certified ones. (ii) By \Cref{thm:incorr} the action is
$A(\mathrm{cell}(\Ss))\in\Us$ for the current map $A$; the monitoring
layer can change which certified $A$ is in force but cannot enlarge the
codomain beyond $\Us$. (iii) Interface switches occur between certified
stable configurations; by standard switched-systems theory a common
Lyapunov function across the finite configuration set, or a minimum dwell
time between switches, guarantees stability of the switched
system~\citep{liberzon2003switching}. Since every one of (i)--(iii)
follows from properties of the control layer's certified configurations
and the interface restriction, and none references any specific
monitoring output, no monitoring behaviour can violate them. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Worst-case: an ordinary stable controller]
\label{cor:worstcase}
Suppose every control-layer virtual channel is fallback-guarded
(\Cref{def:fallback}) and the extended-grid LMIs of
\Cref{prop:virtual-stable} are certified. If the monitoring layer fails
completely---every sensor beyond a single per-channel probe drops out, the
ensemble decoheres to $\Rens\to0$ (\Cref{thm:decohere}), no confirmations
are produced, and every metabolic computation loses its inputs so each
virtual channel reads its fallback $S_v^{0}$---the system reduces to a
cell-partition controller regulating on the surviving physical probe with
all virtual coordinates pinned to their certified-safe fallbacks. That
controller remains globally asymptotically stable, geometrically
convergent, and structurally incorruptible. There is no scenario in which
any monitoring-layer capability, or the validity of any metabolic
computation, is load-bearing for stability or safety.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Total monitoring failure removes all interface requests, leaving the
control layer in its current certified configuration (or, on detected
loss of confirmation, in the certified safe mode via \Cref{prop:dos}).
Loss of all metabolic inputs pins every virtual channel to its fallback
$S_v^{0}$ by \eqref{eq:guarded}, collapsing the state onto a slice whose
cells are certified by \Cref{prop:virtual-stable}. Either configuration is
globally asymptotically stable and incorruptible by
\Cref{thm:additive}(i)--(ii) applied to the extended grid. Regulation
continues from the surviving physical channel's cell membership, which is
all the control law ever required (\Cref{sec:control}). \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Control-layer virtual channels and the worst case]
\label{rem:virtual-worstcase}
A virtual channel that is a genuine control coordinate is, unlike a
monitoring-only confirmation, potentially load-bearing---its \emph{value}
enters the state the controller regulates. The fallback guard
(\Cref{def:fallback}) is precisely what restores the worst-case
guarantee: a failed metabolic script cannot inject a wild coordinate,
only freeze the virtual axis at a certified-safe constant. The worst case
is therefore not ``no virtual channel'' but ``virtual channel pinned to
its safe fallback''---a configuration certified in advance by
\Cref{prop:virtual-stable}. The design licence of \Cref{rem:licence} thus
extends to the metabolic script: it may compute as ambitiously as the
process rewards, because its failure mode is a certified-safe constant,
not an unbounded input.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The design licence]
\label{rem:licence}
\Cref{cor:worstcase} is the reason the monitoring layer may be built
ambitiously. Every elaborate capability---multi-probe confirmation,
phase-locked federation, chromatographic multi-channel fingerprinting of
excursions (\Cref{sec:bioreactor})---is strictly optional from the
control layer's standpoint. If any of it is wrong or absent, the fallback
is not failure but an ordinary, fully-proved controller. The speculative
value of rich monitoring is thus purchased with zero risk to the
guaranteed core: the separation \emph{is} the safety margin.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The safe state}

\begin{definition}[Safe mode]
\label{def:safemode}
The \emph{safe mode} is a distinguished control-layer configuration whose
cell-action map holds the reactor at a conservative, biologically-tolerable
operating point (e.g.\ reduced agitation and feed, temperature and pH held
at culture-safe values), certified stable by its own per-cell LMIs. The
control layer enters safe mode on any of: an interface mode request
(\Cref{def:interface}), a monitoring decline (\Cref{thm:decline}(ii)) for
a safety-relevant excursion, or an internally-detected loss of
confirmation (\Cref{prop:dos}).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Safe mode is reachable without trusted input]
\label{prop:safe-reachable}
Entry to safe mode requires no trusted network input: it is triggered
either by the local monitoring layer's own coherence estimate falling
below threshold, or by the control layer's own timeout on expected
confirmations. Hence a network adversary who suppresses all traffic
causes entry to safe mode, not loss of control.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The triggers in \Cref{def:safemode} are computed locally: the coherence
estimate $\Rens$ from the sensor agents present, and a timeout on the
committed record $M$ (\Cref{def:record}). Neither depends on trusted
inbound content; suppression of traffic drives $\Rens$ down and the
timeout to fire, both of which invoke safe mode by \Cref{def:safemode}.
Safe mode is itself a certified stable configuration
(\Cref{thm:additive}). \qed
\end{proof}

\section{Realisation for a stirred-tank bioreactor}
\label{sec:bioreactor}
% =====================================================================

We now instantiate the framework for a fed-batch stirred-tank reactor, the
workhorse of microbial and cell-culture
bioprocessing~\citep{doran2013bioprocess,shuler2017bioprocess}. The
running example is aerobic fed-batch cultivation of
\textit{Saccharomyces cerevisiae} for biomass or recombinant protein,
whose control is dominated by the interplay of dissolved oxygen, the
respiratory limit, and the feed rate~\citep{sonnleitner1986growth,garciaortega2013fedbatch}.

\subsection{Channels and the S-hypercube}

The five physical channels of \Cref{tab:channels} give a working space
$[0,1]^5$; the virtual metabolic-state channel below extends it to
$\Hs=[0,1]^6$. A representative setpoint for the aerobic growth phase is
$\Sstar$ corresponding to $30\%$ DO, pH $5.0$, $30\,^{\circ}$C, with
agitation and feed as the primary manipulated actions. Manipulated
channels enter both as state coordinates (their current normalised value)
and as the actuation the cell-action map commands; controlled channels
enter as state coordinates whose deviation the map drives to zero.

\subsection{The metabolic script: a virtual respiratory-state channel}
\label{sec:metabolic-script}

The reactor runs two concurrent scripts (\Cref{sec:virtual}). The
\emph{sensor script} produces the five physical coordinates of
\Cref{tab:channels}. The \emph{metabolic script} computes, at a single
node, a sixth coordinate that no probe measures: a \emph{respiratory-state}
channel $S_v$ synthesising the culture's aerobic-versus-overflow metabolic
condition from pH, off-gas $\mathrm{CO_2}$, and temperature. Physically,
aerobic respiration and overflow (fermentative) metabolism differ in their
joint acid/$\mathrm{CO_2}$/thermal signature: overflow onset acidifies the
broth, raises $\mathrm{CO_2}$ evolution disproportionately to $\mathrm{O_2}$
uptake, and shifts heat output~\citep{sonnleitner1986growth}. The node map
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:metabolic-node}
S_v \;=\; g_v\bigl(S_{\mathrm{pH}},\,S_{\mathrm{CO_2}},\,S_{T}\bigr)
\end{equation}
is a fixed, offline-verified Lipschitz combination---increasing in
$\mathrm{CO_2}$ evolution and acidification rate, temperature-compensated
---normalised so that $S_v$ near $0$ is healthy aerobic growth and $S_v$
near $1$ is overflow/oxygen-limited metabolism. No instrument reports
$S_v$; it exists only in the program, yet it is the quantity the operator
most wants to regulate.

By \Cref{def:fallback}, $S_v$ is fallback-guarded: if any of pH,
$\mathrm{CO_2}$, or $T$ is missing or out of range, $S_v$ reads a
conservative fallback $S_v^{0}$ (a value in a cell whose action reduces
feed---the safe metabolic assumption). The extended $[0,1]^6$ grid is
certified by \Cref{prop:virtual-stable}, including the fallback slice, so
the metabolic script can never destabilise the controller.

\paragraph{Regulating the virtual channel.}
Because $S_v$ is a genuine state coordinate (\Cref{prin:obs-comp}), the
cell-action map regulates it directly: cells with high $S_v$ (incipient
overflow) command a feed reduction to bring specific uptake below the
respiratory limit~\citep{sonnleitner1986growth,garciaortega2013fedbatch},
which is precisely the standard fed-batch objective---now driven by a
computed metabolic coordinate rather than by a hand-tuned feed schedule.
The two scripts thus close a loop that no single physical sensor could:
the metabolic script computes the culture's respiratory state, and the
control layer regulates it.

\subsection{The control layer for the reactor}

Cells are axis-aligned boxes on the extended $[0,1]^6$ grid; a modest grid
(e.g.\ $6\times5\times5\times5\times5\times5$ over DO, pH, $T$, agitation,
feed, and the virtual respiratory-state channel) yields $N=18750$ cells,
well within the floor bound $N\le\floor^{-6}$ for realistic probe
resolutions ($\floor\approx0.01$--$0.02$). Each cell stores a nearest-centroid
proportional action \eqref{eq:ncp} mapping DO deviation to agitation and
feed adjustments, pH deviation to acid/base dosing, and temperature
deviation to jacket setpoint. The per-cell LMIs \eqref{eq:lmi} are checked
offline; for the dominant DO/agitation/feed subsystem the linearised
dynamics are a stable first-order oxygen-transfer relation
$\dot{S}_{\mathrm{DO}}=k_La\,(S^{\ast}_{\mathrm{DO}}-S_{\mathrm{DO}})-q_{O_2}X$
whose gain is set by agitation, so the LMI holds whenever the commanded
agitation gain dominates the oxygen-uptake term---exactly the physical
condition for DO controllability. The cell-width bound \eqref{eq:cellbound}
becomes a statement about probe speed versus the oxygen-transfer time
constant, checked at commissioning by \Cref{cor:feasible}.

\subsection{The monitoring layer for the reactor: confirming slices}

The reactor's monitoring layer confirms process events by treating the
sweep of the culture through operating conditions as a sequence of
\emph{slices}---successive cells the state passes through---and
confirming, rather than merely observing, each slice against multiple
independent channels. This is where dissolved oxygen plays a second role.

\paragraph{Dissolved oxygen as a distributed confirmation channel.}
Beyond its controlled-variable role, DO is the most information-rich fast
channel in an aerobic reactor: it responds within seconds to changes in
metabolic activity, mixing, and feed, and can be read by independent
transduction principles---amperometric (Clark-type) and optical
(luminescence-quenching) probes---plus inferred from the off-gas oxygen
balance~\citep{garcia2009review,vanhauwaert2020dosensing}. These are three
\emph{independent} confirmation sources in the sense of
\Cref{thm:multiplicative}, and together they form the coherence triangle
of \Cref{thm:triangle}: a DO excursion confirmed by all three is robust to
any one probe fouling or drifting. The virtual respiratory-state channel
of \Cref{sec:metabolic-script} adds a fourth confirmation source: an
overflow excursion computed from pH/$\mathrm{CO_2}$/$T$ cross-checks a DO
dip, distinguishing a metabolic oxygen-limitation (which the respiratory
channel confirms) from a mechanical mixing failure (which it does not).
Per \Cref{rem:virtual-independence} this fourth leg is independent of the
three DO sources but not of pH/$\mathrm{CO_2}$/$T$; it therefore
strengthens the triangle against DO-probe faults specifically.

\paragraph{A multi-channel excursion fingerprint.}
Each candidate excursion is characterised not by a single retention-like
scalar but by a joint fingerprint across channels: the DO response, the
pH trajectory, the temperature transient, and the off-gas signature. Two
excursions that coincide on one channel (e.g.\ the same DO dip) are
separated by their joint signature across the others, so the monitoring
layer distinguishes, for instance, an oxygen-limitation dip (rising off-gas
$\mathrm{CO_2}$, falling off-gas $\mathrm{O_2}$, biomass-driven) from a
mixing-failure dip (localised, agitation-correlated, no off-gas shift).
The distinguishing power grows multiplicatively with the number of
independent channels folded in (\Cref{thm:multiplicative,thm:admit}),
which is the concrete payoff of federating sensors rather than trusting
one.

\paragraph{The federated sensor ensemble.}
The reactor's probes are the temporal agents of \Cref{def:agent}. When the
probe set is homogeneous and healthy, the ensemble phase-locks
(\Cref{thm:critical}) and confirmations are free (\Cref{thm:zero}); a DO
excursion is confirmed three ways with no coordination overhead. When a
probe fouls or the timing degrades, the ensemble decoheres gracefully
(\Cref{thm:decohere}) and confirmation continues at reduced resolution.

\paragraph{An ensemble invariant for trust.}
Alongside the local respiratory-state channel (which the reactor uses to
\emph{act}), the metabolic script computes an \emph{ensemble invariant}
(\Cref{def:ensemble-invariant}) that the reactor uses to \emph{trust}: a
collective DO-consistency quantity---for instance the phase coherence
$\Rens$ of the DO-related oscillators (\Cref{ex:order-invariant}), or a
symmetric agreement measure across the amperometric, optical, and off-gas
DO estimates. No single DO probe carries this quantity, yet every healthy
probe is consistent with it; it is true of the whole DO ensemble and
present in none of its members. By \Cref{prop:invariant-robust} a single
fouled or spoofed DO probe cannot move it by more than $O(1/n)$, so a
\emph{drop} in the invariant is a reliable, unit-independent signal that
the DO ensemble as a whole is losing coherence---a fault that no
individual probe reading would reveal, because each probe still reports a
plausible value on its own. The invariant thus detects exactly the
correlated-fault case that the independence caveat of
\Cref{rem:virtual-independence} warns about.

\subsection{The two layers in operation}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Nominal.} Two scripts run concurrently: the sensor script
produces the five physical coordinates and the metabolic script computes
the virtual respiratory-state coordinate $S_v$
(\Cref{sec:metabolic-script}). The control layer regulates DO, pH,
temperature, \emph{and} $S_v$ to setpoint by cell dispatch on the extended
$[0,1]^6$ grid. The monitoring layer, with a coherent probe ensemble,
confirms that the state remains in its intended cells and that no
excursion fingerprint has appeared. No interface message is sent; the
controller runs on cell membership alone.
\item \textbf{Confirmed excursion.} An oxygen-limitation fingerprint is
confirmed by the DO triangle plus off-gas (\Cref{thm:decline}(i)). The
monitoring layer issues a \emph{setpoint request}
(\Cref{def:interface}(i))---e.g.\ reduce feed to bring uptake below the
respiratory limit~\citep{sonnleitner1986growth}. The control layer
switches to the corresponding certified configuration; stability is
preserved by \Cref{thm:additive}.
\item \textbf{Contested excursion.} The amperometric and optical DO probes
disagree beyond reconciliation and off-gas is ambiguous
(\Cref{thm:decline}(ii)). The monitoring layer \emph{declines}, names the
conflicting classes, and---because the excursion is safety-relevant---issues
a \emph{mode request} for safe mode (\Cref{def:safemode}). The control
layer enters the certified safe configuration.
\item \textbf{Monitoring and metabolic-script failure.} Probes drop out,
the ensemble decoheres, and the metabolic script loses its inputs so $S_v$
reads its safe fallback $S_v^{0}$ (feed-reducing). By
\Cref{cor:worstcase} the reactor continues under cell-partition control on
the surviving DO probe with the virtual axis pinned to its
certified-safe fallback, globally asymptotically stable and incorruptible,
exactly as an ordinary controller would---which is the guarantee the whole
architecture is built to provide.
\end{itemize}

\section{Algorithms}
\label{sec:algorithms}
% =====================================================================

We give the six runtime algorithms. \Cref{alg:control,alg:lmi} constitute
the control layer; \Cref{alg:cusum,alg:confirm,alg:coherence,alg:decohere}
constitute the monitoring layer. Note the strict flow: the control loop
(\Cref{alg:control}) never reads a monitoring output directly---it reads
only the current certified configuration, which the interface may have
switched.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Control-layer step (runtime)}
\label{alg:control}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require S-state $\Ss$; active cell-action map $A$ (certified);
committed record $M$
\Ensure control action $\mathbf u\in\Us$
\State $i \gets \mathrm{cell}(\Ss)$
\Comment{$O(m)$ axis comparisons}
\State $\mathbf u \gets A(C_i)$
\Comment{lookup in pre-approved $\Us$}
\State apply $\mathbf u$ to actuators (saturating at channel limits)
\State $M \gets M+1$
\Comment{monotone record, \Cref{def:record}}
\State \Return $\mathbf u$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Offline stability certification}
\label{alg:lmi}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require partition $\Cs$; setpoint $\Sstar$; gain $K$; plant Jacobians
$\{\mathbf A_i\}$
\Ensure \textsc{certified} or \textsc{reject}
\For{each cell $C_i \in \Cs$}
  \State solve LMI $\mathbf A_i+\mathbf A_i^{\top}\preceq-\alpha_i I$,
         $\alpha_i>0$
  \Comment{\Cref{cor:lmi}}
  \If{infeasible} \State \Return \textsc{reject} \EndIf
\EndFor
\State verify $\floor\le\wcell\le 1/(2\Bproc)$
\Comment{\Cref{thm:nyquist}}
\State verify $h< 2\min_i\alpha_i / L_F^2$
\Comment{\Cref{thm:banach}}
\State \Return \textsc{certified}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Excursion detection (per channel, CUSUM)}
\label{alg:cusum}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require deviation $\DP(k)$; limit $H=\wcell/2$; running $C^{\pm}$
\Ensure alarm flag
\State $C^{+} \gets \max(0,\,C^{+}+\DP(k))$
\State $C^{-} \gets \min(0,\,C^{-}+\DP(k))$
\If{$C^{+}>H$ \textbf{or} $C^{-}<-H$}
  \State \Return \textsc{alarm}
  \Comment{cell-crossing, \Cref{thm:cusum}}
\EndIf
\State \Return \textsc{in-cell}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Confirmation with confirm-or-decline}
\label{alg:confirm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require candidate excursion $x$; source set $\Gamma$ (finite)
\Ensure \textsc{confirmed}$(c)$ or \textsc{decline}$(\{c_j\})$
\State $\mathcal K \gets \varnothing$
\Comment{reached outcome classes}
\For{each source $\gamma \in \Gamma$}
  \State $c \gets$ outcome class of $\gamma$ on $x$
  \State $\mathcal K \gets \mathcal K \cup \{c\}$
\EndFor
\Comment{closure by exhaustion, \Cref{thm:decline}}
\If{$|\mathcal K| = 1$} \State \Return \textsc{confirmed}$(\mathcal K)$
\Else \State \Return \textsc{decline}$(\mathcal K)$ \EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Coherence check (sign-only)}
\label{alg:coherence}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require sources $\Gamma$; pairwise support signs $s_{ij}\in\{+,-\}$
\Ensure \textsc{coherent} or \textsc{unfounded}
\State build support graph $G$: edge $j\to i$ iff $s_{ij}=+$
\If{$G$ has a mutually-supporting cycle of length $\ge 3$}
  \State \Return \textsc{coherent}
  \Comment{\Cref{thm:triangle,cor:signonly}}
\Else \State \Return \textsc{unfounded} \EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Ensemble coherence and graceful decoherence}
\label{alg:decohere}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require sensor phases $\{\phi_j\}$; lock threshold $\theta=0.95$
\Ensure regime; safe-mode trigger if needed
\State $\Rens \gets \frac1n\bigl|\sum_j e^{i\phi_j}\bigr|$
\Comment{\Cref{def:order}}
\If{$\Rens \ge \theta$} \State regime $\gets$ \textsc{phase-locked}
  \Comment{free confirmation, \Cref{thm:zero}}
\ElsIf{$\Rens$ dropped below prior regime}
  \State escalate coordination cost (\Cref{thm:decohere})
  \If{$\Rens$ below safe threshold \textbf{or} confirmation timeout}
    \State issue \textsc{mode-request}(safe)
    \Comment{\Cref{prop:safe-reachable}}
  \EndIf
\EndIf
\State \Return regime
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Sensor synthesis (time$\leftrightarrow$frequency)}
\label{alg:synth}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require input S-values $S_1,\dots,S_r$; weights $a_j$ ($\sum a_j=1$);
concentration floor $\eta$; fallback $S_v^0$
\Ensure synthesised value $S_\star$; concentration $\Rstar$
\For{$j = 1$ \textbf{to} $r$}
  \State $\phi_j \gets 2\pi S_j$
  \Comment{switch to frequency domain, \Cref{def:phase}}
\EndFor
\State $z \gets \sum_{j} a_j\,e^{i\phi_j}$
\Comment{mean-recovery resultant, \eqref{eq:phasemean}}
\State $\Rstar \gets |z|$
\Comment{concentration = ensemble invariant, \Cref{prop:synth-invariant}}
\If{$\Rstar \ge \eta$}
  \State $\phi_\star \gets \arg(z)$; \quad $S_\star \gets \phi_\star / 2\pi$
  \Comment{switch back, $\Phi^{-1}$}
\Else
  \State $S_\star \gets S_v^0$
  \Comment{inputs too dispersed: fallback, \Cref{prop:synth-wd}}
\EndIf
\State \Return $S_\star$, $\Rstar$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Validation protocol}
\label{sec:validation}
% =====================================================================

The theorems are proved from the definitions and need no simulation to
hold. It is nonetheless essential, before deployment, to confirm that a
concrete grid and plant model satisfy the certificate conditions and that
the layered behaviour is as claimed. We specify a validation protocol in
three parts; it is designed so that each check maps to a specific theorem
and can be run offline (control layer) or in a hardware-in-the-loop rig
(monitoring layer).

\subsection{Control-layer certification}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{V\arabic*.},leftmargin=2.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Floor and cell count.} Measure each probe's resolution
$\delta_k$, form $\floor=\min_k\delta_k$, and verify the chosen grid has
$\wcell\ge\floor$ and $N\le\floor^{-m}$ (\Cref{prop:floor,lem:finite-cells}).
\item \textbf{Per-cell LMIs.} For the identified cellwise Jacobians
$\{\mathbf A_i\}$, solve \eqref{eq:lmi} for every cell
(\Cref{alg:lmi}); certification requires all $N$ LMIs feasible
(\Cref{cor:lmi}).
\item \textbf{Sampling feasibility.} Estimate the closed-loop bandwidth
$\Bproc$ from the oxygen-transfer and metabolic time constants and verify
$\floor\le1/(2\Bproc)$ (\Cref{cor:feasible}); if it fails, the remedy is a
faster probe, per \Cref{cor:feasible}.
\item \textbf{Contraction rate.} Verify $h<2\min_i\alpha_i/L_F^2$ and
record the predicted rate $\rho$ (\Cref{thm:banach}); check that
simulated step responses contract at rate $\le\rho^n$.
\item \textbf{Configuration switching.} For every pre-approved setpoint
and the safe mode, repeat V2 and verify a common Lyapunov function or a
dwell-time bound across the configuration set (\Cref{thm:additive}(iii)).
\item \textbf{Virtual-channel certification.} Verify the extended-grid
LMIs including the fallback slice $\{S_v=S_v^{0}\}$
(\Cref{prop:virtual-stable}), and confirm that forcing $\mathrm{ok}_v$
false pins $S_v$ to $S_v^{0}$ and holds the reactor in a certified-safe
configuration.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Monitoring-layer characterisation}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{V\arabic*.},leftmargin=2.4em,itemsep=2pt,start=7]
\item \textbf{CUSUM operating point.} Inject known DO excursions and verify
that cell-boundary crossings coincide with CUSUM alarms at limit
$H=\wcell/2$, and that the in-control average run length matches the
predicted function of $H$ (\Cref{thm:cusum,cor:arl}).
\item \textbf{Composition.} With independent DO channels (amperometric,
optical, off-gas), verify that combined confirmation power follows
$1-\prod_i(1-\kappa_i)$ (\Cref{thm:multiplicative}) and that a coherent
triangle survives removal of any one channel (\Cref{thm:triangle}).
\item \textbf{Decline.} Force amperometric/optical DO disagreement and
verify the monitor declines and requests safe mode rather than picking a
channel (\Cref{thm:decline}(ii)).
\item \textbf{Federation.} Vary timing coupling and probe homogeneity;
verify the phase-lock transition at $\Kc=2\sigma_\omega/\pi$
(\Cref{thm:critical}) and zero-overhead confirmation above
$\Rens=0.95$ (\Cref{thm:zero}).
\item \textbf{Ensemble invariant.} Verify the computed collective
DO-consistency invariant is unchanged under permutation of interchangeable
DO probes (relabelling-invariance) and that a single spoofed or fouled
probe shifts it by at most $O(1/n)$
(\Cref{def:ensemble-invariant,prop:invariant-robust}), while a
\emph{correlated} multi-probe fault does move it---confirming it detects
the failure mode redundant probes miss.
\item \textbf{Sensor synthesis round-trip.} Verify the time--frequency
switch is lossless to numerical precision ($\Phi^{-1}(\Phi(S))=S$,
\Cref{prop:switch}) and cell-preserving (\Cref{prop:switch-cells}); that
the synthesised value and concentration of \Cref{thm:synthesis} match the
circular-mean formula \eqref{eq:phasemean}; and that dispersed inputs
($\Rstar<\eta$) trigger the fallback (\Cref{prop:synth-wd,alg:synth}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The worst-case drill}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{V\arabic*.},leftmargin=2.4em,itemsep=2pt,start=12]
\item \textbf{Total monitoring failure.} Disable all probes but one, force
ensemble decoherence, and suppress all confirmations. Verify the reactor
continues under single-channel cell-partition control, that regulation
error still contracts at the certified rate, and that no action outside
$\Us$ ever occurs (\Cref{cor:worstcase}). This is the decisive test: the
system must behave as an ordinary stable controller, confirming that the
monitoring layer was additive throughout.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[What a pass means]
\label{rem:pass}
V1--V6 passing certifies the control layer independently of any monitoring
capability. V7--V11 characterise the monitoring layer's added value
(confirmation, federation, the ensemble invariant, and the synthesis
round-trip) without being required for V1--V6. V12, the worst-case drill,
confirms the separation empirically: with the monitoring layer entirely
removed, the V1--V6 guarantees still hold. A deployment is licensed on
V1--V6 and V12 alone; the remaining checks quantify the optional benefit.
\end{remark}

Three findings sharpen the paper's own claims rather than merely
restating them.

\emph{The $\alpha$--$L_F$ distinction is load-bearing
(\Cref{fig:panel1}).} The Banach constant of \Cref{thm:banach} uses the
\emph{norm} descent rate $\alpha=-\lambda_{\max}(\mathbf A+\mathbf
A^{\top})/2$, which is half the descent rate of the quadratic Lyapunov
function itself. Conflating the two makes the predicted $\rho$ imaginary
for well-damped plants; using the correct $\alpha$ yields, for the yeast
DO/agitation/feed Jacobian, $\alpha=1.25\le L_F=1.62$ and a predicted
$\rho=0.638$ that genuinely dominates both the exact one-step operator
norm $\lVert I+h\mathbf A\rVert_2=0.408$ and the measured contraction
$0.403$. The theorem holds precisely because the hypothesis $\alpha\le
L_F$ is respected.

\emph{Finite-$n$ Kuramoto undershoot is real and bounded
(\Cref{fig:panel2}).} At $n=400$ probes and $K=3\Kc$ the simulated order
parameter is $\Rens=0.65$, below the thermodynamic-limit branch
$\Rens^{\ast}=\sqrt{1-\Kc/K}=0.816$; the transition is monotone in $K$
and rises toward the branch as $K$ grows. This is exactly the finite-size
correction flagged in \Cref{sec:discussion}: the monitoring layer's
coherence is a graded, correctly-ordered quantity, and---crucially---no
control-layer guarantee depends on its exact value.

\emph{Identities hold to machine precision; the invariant catches what
redundancy misses (\Cref{fig:panel3,fig:panel4}).} The
time--frequency switch and the circular-mean synthesis are exact to
$\sim10^{-16}$, confirming they lose no information. The ensemble
invariant is unchanged under probe permutation and moves by at most
$0.10$ under any single-unit spoof (within the $O(1/n)$ band), yet a
\emph{correlated} three-probe fault shifts it by $0.39$---so the
invariant detects precisely the shared-mode failure that independent
redundancy cannot, as \Cref{rem:virtual-independence} predicts. Finally,
under $500$ steps of adversarial payloads (random, out-of-range,
$\pm\infty$, NaN), not one dispatched action left the $46{,}656$-element
pre-approved set $\Us$, and in the worst-case drill---monitoring removed,
the virtual axis pinned to its safe fallback---the loop still contracted
to the setpoint (final error $3\times10^{-12}$) with zero action-set
escapes, the empirical form of \Cref{cor:worstcase}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{What is guaranteed, and at what strength}

The control-layer results---S-normalisation, the cell-partition law,
piecewise Lyapunov stability reducible to per-cell LMIs, Banach
contraction, the Nyquist-type cell-width bound, and structural
incorruptibility---are proved from the definitions using standard
theorems, and are the load-bearing core. They depend only on the
resolution floor $\floor>0$ (\Cref{prop:floor}), forward invariance of
$\Hs$ (\Cref{ass:dynamics}, physically guaranteed by actuator saturation),
and the cellwise-Lipschitz plant model. A deployment stands or falls on
these.

The monitoring-layer results---the CUSUM identity, multiplicative
composition, the coherence triangle, the confirm-or-decline dichotomy, and
sensor federation---are equally proved, but they are, by design,
\emph{additive}: \Cref{thm:additive} shows none of them is load-bearing
for stability or safety. This asymmetry is deliberate. We place the
strongest guarantees where failure is intolerable (regulation and
integrity) and the richest, more model-dependent machinery where failure
is merely a loss of diagnostic resolution.

\subsection{Honest limitations}

We mark the assumptions plainly.
\emph{(i)} The stability certificate depends on an identified
cellwise-Lipschitz plant model; a badly wrong model can pass the LMIs yet
mis-predict the true dynamics. The mitigation is that the worst-case
fallback (\Cref{cor:worstcase}) is a conservative single-channel
controller, and the safe mode (\Cref{def:safemode}) is chosen to be
biologically tolerable independent of model fidelity.
\emph{(ii)} The Kuramoto results of \Cref{sec:federation} are large-ensemble
mean-field statements; for a handful of probes ($n<20$) the critical
coupling and order-parameter relations carry finite-size corrections. We
therefore never let control-layer behaviour depend on exact coherence
values---coherence only gates \emph{how cheaply} the monitoring layer
confirms, never \emph{whether} the controller is stable.
\emph{(iii)} The confirmation-power model \eqref{eq:mult} assumes source
independence; correlated probe faults (a common contamination fouling
multiple probes) violate it, which is exactly why the coherence triangle
(\Cref{thm:triangle}) demands \emph{independent} channels and why decline
(\Cref{thm:decline}) is a first-class outcome.
\emph{(iv)} The incorruptibility argument (\Cref{thm:incorr}) is
structural: it eliminates the payload-to-plant path but does not replace
network hardening against denial of service, which it instead converts
into a detectable, safe-mode-triggering event (\Cref{prop:dos,prop:safe-reachable}).

\subsection{Relation to established practice}

The control layer is a quantised feedback
controller~\citep{brockett2000quantized} on a normalised state space, with
a piecewise Lyapunov certificate~\citep{johansson1998computation,liberzon2003switching};
its novelty is the S-normalisation that unifies heterogeneous bioprocess
channels and the arrangement that makes it separable from monitoring. The
monitoring layer's excursion detection is CUSUM~\citep{page1954continuous,montgomery2020spc}
recast as cell-crossing, and its federation is Kuramoto
synchronisation~\citep{strogatz2000kuramoto,acebron2005kuramoto} recast as
sensor agreement; the contribution is to place these strictly downstream of
a proved controller behind a one-directional interface. The soft-sensor
and process-analytical-technology
literature~\citep{luttmann2012softsensors,lourenco2012bioprocess,fda2004pat}
has long argued for richer monitoring; our contribution is to make that
richness safe by construction through the separation of \Cref{sec:separation}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

We have presented a bioreactor control system organised around a strict
separation between a provably-stable control layer and an additive
monitoring layer, joined by a one-directional interface. The control
layer normalises every heterogeneous channel onto a bounded hypercube,
partitions it into finitely many cells above a resolution floor, and
dispatches a pre-compiled action from the occupied cell. This austere
design is globally asymptotically stable (piecewise Lyapunov, reducible to
per-cell LMIs), geometrically convergent (Banach contraction), correctly
sampled (a Nyquist-type cell-width bound), and structurally incorruptible
(no payload can drive an action outside the pre-approved set). The
monitoring layer treats each measurement as one confirmation in a
redundant, federated ensemble: excursions are detected as cell-crossings
(equivalently, CUSUM alarms), confirmed multiplicatively across
independent channels, grounded only when a coherence triangle of at least
three independent sources agrees, and honestly declined to a safe state
when they do not; sensors federate by phase entrainment, confirming for
free at coherence and degrading gracefully otherwise.

The organising theorem is the separation (\Cref{thm:additive}) and its
corollary (\Cref{cor:worstcase}): because the interface is one-directional
and the control law never references a monitoring output, the monitoring
layer can only ever improve the system, never break it, and in the worst
case---every probe lost, the ensemble fully decohered, all confirmation
silenced---the reactor is controlled by an ordinary, fully-proved
single-channel cell-partition controller. That is the design's central
promise: one may build the monitoring layer as ambitiously as the process
rewards, because its failure is bounded below by a guaranteed stable
controller. In the worst case, you just control the process, as always.
